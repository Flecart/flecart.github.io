Gaussians are one of the most important family of probability distributions. They arise naturally in the law of large numbers and have some nice properties that we will briefly present and prove here in this note. They are also quite common for Gaussian Processes and the Clustering algorithm. They have also something to say about Maximum Entropy Principle. The best thing if you want to learn this part actually well is section 2.3 of (Bishop 2006), so go there my friend :)

The Density Function#

The single variable Gaussian is as follows:

N(μ,σ)=2π​σ1​exp(−2σ2(x−μ)2​)

This can be generalized for the multi variable case

N(μ,Σ)=(2π)d/2∣Σ∣​1​exp(−21​(x−μ)TΣ−1(x−μ))

Where d is the dimensionality for the multidimensional Gaussian.

Integral is 1#

We now prove that the integral of the Gaussian PDF is 1, this is a requirements needed to be considered a probability distribution function.

First, let's prove a famous equality:

I=∫−∞∞​exp(−x2)dx=π​

This is kinda surprising, we need some care to prove it:

I2​=∫−∞∞​exp(−x2−y2)dxdy=∫02π​∫0∞​rexp(−r2)drdθ=2π⋅(−21​)∫0∞​−2rexp(−r2)dr=−22π​exp(−r2)​0∞​=π⟹I=π​​​

Note that in the second step we changed variables: This is quite interesting. If we do the same derivation with exp(−2σ2(x−μ)2​), first doing a change of variables y=(x−μ) where we have dy=dx, then doing another change of variables z=2σ2​y​ where we get 2σ2​dz=dy now we have the same integral, plus an added constant multiplicative term. So we have

∫−∞∞​exp(−2σ2(x−μ)2​)dx=2σ2​∫−∞∞​exp(−z2)dz=2πσ2​

Which finishes the derivation of the normalizing term.

Error Function#

Sometimes, for example calculating the mean of the folded Gaussian, is useful to consider the error function. This is defined as

erf(x)=π​2​∫0x​exp(−t2)dt

Sometimes this is also written, using the symmetry over the x−axis as

erf(x)=π​1​∫−xx​exp(−t2)dt

We observe that the limit x→+∞ is 1, and that x→−∞ is -1.

Another useful relation is with the Gaussian CDF:

erf(2​x​)=2Φ(x)−1

We also note that it is anti-symmetric:

erf(−x)=−erf(x)

Some properties of Gaussians#

The conditional Gaussian#

If we have X,Y which are jointly Gaussian, then the distribution p(X=x∣Y=y) is a gaussian with the following mean and variance:

μX∣Y=y​=μX​+ΣXY​ΣYY−1​(y−μY​)

And

ΣX∣Y​=ΣXX​−ΣXY​ΣYY−1​ΣYX​

The proof is presented in section 2.3 of (Bishop 2006)

Gaussian characteristic function#

Characteristic functions are sometimes useful to prove that two distributions are the same as each other. One can prove that the characteristic function for Gaussians is

E[exp(itX)]=exp(μit−21​tTΣt)

Let's prove the uni-variate case, we will see that it will be exactly this value. We need to compute the value:

E[exp(itX)]=∫−∞∞​2π​σ1​exp(−2σ2(x−μ)2​+itx)dx

The idea is to complete the square, and the by knowing the value of the integral of the completed square, we simplify.

∫−∞∞​2π​σ1​exp(−2σ2(x−μ)2​+itx)dx=∫−∞∞​2π​σ1​exp(−2σ2(x−(μ+σ2it))2−2μitσ2+σ4t2​)dx=exp(μit−21​σ2t2)⋅∫−∞∞​2π​σ1​exp(−2σ2(x−(μ+σ2it))2​)dx=exp(μit−21​σ2t2)​​

Sum Gaussians are Gaussian#

This is easily provable, if we have X∼N(μX​,ΣX​) and a compatible distribution Y∼N(μY​,ΣY​) then we have that the distribution X+Y=N(μX​+μY​,ΣX​+ΣY​) The proof should use characteristic functions in the line of linear Gaussians.

E[exp(it(X+Y))]​=E[exp(itX)]E[exp(itY)]=exp(μX​it−21​tTΣX​t)exp(μY​it−21​tTΣY​t)=exp((μX​+μY​)it−21​tT(ΣX​+ΣY​)t)​​

Which finishes the proof. One can also extend this result to every linear combination of Gaussians.

Properties to remember#

  • Compact representation of high dimensional joint distributions: instead of using 2n variables we just need n2, this is why Gaussian Processes are analytically handy.
  • Closed form inference (I think about the Conjugacy of itself, this is because Gaussians are in the The Exponential Family.)

Confidence Intervals#

Gaussians are a nice distribution. We have listed many of its properties by now. But one of the most over-utilized feature is the ease in computing 1−α confidence intervals where α is called significance level: meaning we want to find the interval where our prediction lies there with 1−α probability. This is usually easy to compute with z tables. The Standard Error of a Gaussian is n​σ​ and is related to the square root of the mean variance.

So after we have computed this values, the confidence interval for a prediction is just

xˉ±z⋅SE

Where xˉ is the expected value for our prediction.

General KL divergence between Gaussians#

The KL divergence between two Gaussians is given by:

KL(p∣∣q)=21​(log∣Σp​∣∣Σq​∣​−d+tr(Σq−1​Σp​)+(μq​−μp​)TΣq−1​(μq​−μp​))

This is a good resource for a proof.

Forward KL#

One can prove that the forward KL divergence between two Gaussians defined as p∼N(μ1​,diag{σ12​,…,σd2​}) and q=N(0,1) is given by:

KL(p∣∣q)=21​i=1∑d​(σi2​+μi2​−logσi2​−1)

Let’s interpret this. The μ term works to pull the mean toward zero. The σ term introduces a penalty for high variance values, while the logσ2 term imposes a cost for low values of σ. This forward KL is what is used for Autoencoders.

Reverse KL#

Given the same assumptions we have that the KL of q over p is given by:

KL(q∣∣p)=21​i=1∑d​(σi2​μi2​​+σi−2​+logσi2​−1)
