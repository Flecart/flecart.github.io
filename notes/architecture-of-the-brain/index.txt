<h4 id="small-comparison-with-computers">Small comparison with Computers<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#small-comparison-with-computers">#</a></h4>
<p>A gross observation between computer's transistors and human neurons is that there a big difference of numbers:</p>
<ul>
<li>trillions of <strong>transistors</strong> vs billions of <strong>neurons</strong>.</li>
<li>6 orders of magnitude <strong>frequency</strong> difference (Ghz versus 1kHz for neurons).</li>
<li>Many many <strong>neural types</strong> and different types of <strong>connections</strong>.</li>
<li>And the digital vs analog and chemical modes of communication.</li>
<li><strong>Parallel</strong> processor abilities.</li>
<li>Fixed vs plastic architectures
But this is comparing with transistors with one higher level object, so this comparison might not be completely fair. They are very different from this point of view.</li>
</ul>
<p>And only some brain areas are similar to real neural networks.</p>
<h3 id="david-marrs-tri-level-approach">David Marr's Tri-level approach<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#david-marrs-tri-level-approach">#</a></h3>
<p>We don't follow now this approach, a clean separation of the levels is not exactly clear.
There are some regions that we don't know what they are doing at all. Internal state with goal is important, most of the computer systems do not have this internal driver.</p>
<p>See <a href="https://snastase.github.io/files/Marr_1982.pdf">original paper</a>.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>This revolution stemmed from physiological work and makes us realizethat the activity of each single neuron may play a significant role in perception,</p>
</blockquote>
<h4 id="the-tri-level">The Tri-level<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#the-tri-level">#</a></h4>
<p><strong>Computational Level:</strong> what kinds of computation is the system doing? Why does it do these things (highest level things) (e.g. intentions and beliefs with ToM).</p>
<p><strong>Algorithmic Representational Level</strong>: how does the system do what it does? What processes does it effectively manipulate?</p>
<p><strong>Physical level</strong>: how does the system implement it on the physical level (chemical or physical processes of brain firing).</p>
<p>For the brain, the first level it is debatable (not clear what the brain is trying to solve), and the representational level is just incomprehensible. Neuroscientists usually just measure at the physical/hardware level.</p>
<h4 id="what-does-the-brain-do">What does the Brain do?<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#what-does-the-brain-do">#</a></h4>
<p>This attempts to answer the computational level problem:
Some debated things are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Brain creates a <strong>model of the environment</strong>.
<ul>
<li>Having models of the world is useful to predict what they are likely to do and plan accordingly.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>The brain makes internal decisions on different time scales (goals).</li>
<li>This internal decisions (intents) are used to make actions.
<ul>
<li>We want to predict the consequence of our actions on the environment.</li>
<li>Has <strong>intentions</strong> or goals and acts on it.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>It is able to <em>evaluate their actions</em> (perhaps survival, safety, or other details) and update the internal model of the world.</li>
<li><strong>Predictive models are useful</strong>, to plan and act on this information (example is gazelle chase for predators)</li>
</ul>
<p>And we would ask how the brain does all this.</p>
<h4 id="the-development-of-the-brain">The Development of the Brain<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#the-development-of-the-brain">#</a></h4>
<p>It first starts with an elongated <strong>neural tube</strong> (this is the precursor.
The different parts of development are named by the stage of development, also related to the evolution steps.
It is very similar across vertebrates.
There are three different development states after the formation of the neural tube.</p>
<ol>
<li>Very long tube that then differentiates and becomes bigger and bigger. Diencephalon enlarges. They all form with the same similar process.
<img src="/images/notes/Architecture of the Brain-20250817135003478.webp" style="width: 100%" class="center" alt="Architecture of the Brain-20250817135003478"></li>
</ol>
<h3 id="parts-of-the-brain">Parts of the brain<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#parts-of-the-brain">#</a></h3>
<p>Most of what we know about the cortex has been discovered by injuries on those parts of the brain. Now  we now can use reversible optogenetics to check some parts of the brain precisely: activation/inhibition and recording of brain circuits.</p>
<p>We can now use electrodes in the brain: Deep Brain Stimulation (DBS)(Parkinson’s, Depression, OCD, Epilepsy, ...), or subcutaneus parts, or transcranical stimulation bymagnetic/electric fields or ultrasound waves, that can excite some brain areas (not very specific, but not invasive).</p>

<h4 id="modulating-brain-areas">Modulating Brain Areas<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#modulating-brain-areas">#</a></h4>
<p><strong>Neuron-Specific Drug Delivery via Focused Ultrasound Uncaging</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Principle</strong>: drugs are packaged in carriers (e.g., nanoparticles, liposomes) that remain inactive (“caged”).</li>
<li>Focused ultrasound breaks or heats carriers at target sites → <strong>local drug release only where ultrasound is applied</strong>.</li>
<li><strong>Advantages</strong>:
<ul>
<li>High spatial precision.</li>
<li>Reduces systemic side effects.</li>
<li>Can deliver neuromodulators, neurotransmitters, or other neuron-specific agents.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Deep Brain Simulation</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Invasive</strong>: electrodes surgically implanted into specific brain nuclei.</li>
<li>Provides <strong>continuous or patterned electrical stimulation</strong>.</li>
<li><strong>Clinical uses</strong>:
<ul>
<li>Parkinson’s disease (reduce tremors, motor symptoms).</li>
<li>Depression (treatment-resistant).</li>
<li>OCD.</li>
<li>Epilepsy.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Advantage: highly effective in movement disorders.</li>
<li>Limitation: requires neurosurgery, risk of infection, electrode misplacement.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Transcranial Stimulation</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Uses <strong>magnetic, electric, or ultrasound fields</strong> applied through the skull.</li>
<li><strong>Techniques</strong>:
<ul>
<li><strong>TMS (Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation)</strong> → magnetic pulses induce currents in brain tissue.</li>
<li><strong>tDCS / tACS (Transcranial Direct/Alternating Current Stimulation)</strong> → low current applied via scalp electrodes.</li>
<li><strong>tFUS (Transcranial Focused Ultrasound Stimulation)</strong> → acoustic waves target deep brain regions with high spatial precision.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Applications</strong>: Modulate cortical excitability, study brain–behavior relationships, potential therapy for depression, chronic pain, stroke recovery.</li>
</ul>

<p>The types of cells are different, so the cortices are specialized in this manner, but are able to adapt if their input is different (remember the example of the replaced auditory cortex for visual cortex.) The other is called <strong>electrical stimulation mapping</strong>.</p>

<li>Speech processing (Wernicke) is close to speech production (Broca)</li>

<h4 id="cytoarchitectonics">Cytoarchitectonics<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#cytoarchitectonics">#</a></h4>
<p>Another method to identify brain areas is to see at the <strong>cytoarchitectural</strong> structure of the cells:
<img src="/images/notes/Architecture of the Brain-20250818215639191.webp" width="561" class="center" alt="Architecture of the Brain-20250818215639191"></p>
<ul>
<li>Pioneered by <strong>Korbinian Brodmann</strong> → “Brodmann areas.”</li>
<li>Method: study the <strong>size, density, and layering of neurons</strong> under a microscope using histological stains (e.g., Nissl stain).</li>
<li>Contribution: reveals systematic differences in cortical layers (e.g., primary sensory cortex has a <strong>thick Layer IV</strong> for thalamic input; motor cortex has <strong>giant pyramidal cells in Layer V</strong>).</li>
<li>This allows researchers to define distinct <strong>structural “maps”</strong> of cortex even without knowing function.</li>
</ul>
<p>There is also a close friend called <strong>Myeloarchitectonics (fibers</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Method: stain for <strong>myelinated axons</strong> (e.g., using Weigert stains).</li>
<li>Contribution: highlights the <strong>density and pattern of intracortical connections</strong>.</li>
<li>Different areas show unique myelination patterns — for instance, <strong>primary visual cortex (V1)</strong> has a prominent <strong>stria of Gennari</strong> (a dense band of myelinated fibers in Layer IV).</li>
<li>This gives an anatomical signature distinguishing one cortical area from another.</li>
</ul>
<h4 id="cytoarchitectornics-of-motor-and-sensory-cortex">Cytoarchitectornics of Motor and Sensory Cortex<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#cytoarchitectornics-of-motor-and-sensory-cortex">#</a></h4>
<p>For example:
<strong>Motor cortex:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>The primary motor cortex (M1) is heavily involved in sending commands to the spinal cord and brainstem to drive movement.</li>
<li>Layer V of M1 is packed with <strong>large pyramidal neurons</strong>, particularly the <strong>Betz cells</strong>, which are some of the largest neurons in the brain.</li>
<li>These neurons project <strong>directly to the spinal cord (corticospinal tract)</strong>, which is why Layer V is so prominent.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Sensory cortex:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Primary sensory areas, like the primary somatosensory cortex (S1), primarily <strong>receive input from the thalamus</strong>.</li>
<li>Thalamic projections mostly terminate in <strong>Layer IV</strong>, which is why Layer IV is thick in sensory cortices and relatively thin in motor cortex.</li>
<li>The sensory cortex then processes and distributes this input to other layers for integration and output.</li>
</ul>

<h4 id="the-striatum">The striatum<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#the-striatum">#</a></h4>
<p>The striatum area seems to converge processing from the dopaminergic pathways and the cortical areas.
Medium spiny neurons form <em>trisynaptic</em> connections with the cortex and the VTA (ventral tegmental area).</p>
<ul>
<li>If the dopamine binds to D1 receptors, it becomes weaker, if D2 the connection becomes stronger. D1 stimulates movement while D2 inhibits it.</li>
<li>It starts with very complex signal and ends up with a few actuator neurons (some thousands).</li>
</ul>
<p>The learning loops becomes shorter with time, (From <strong>conscious learning</strong> to <strong>unconscious execution</strong>).</p>
<p>There are mainly two pathways in the striatum, the ventral and the dorsal one.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Ventral</strong>: (Nucleus accumbens), is the main region that gets dopamine dignals from the VTA section, this is why we assume this section does reward computation.</li>
<li><strong>Dorsal</strong>: It has controls to the motor and cognitive control parts, so this part decides the <em>actions</em> to take.</li>
</ul>
<p>And the major <strong>inputs</strong> to striatum are:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Cortex (glutamate → excitatory).</strong></li>
<li><strong>Thalamus (glutamate).</strong></li>
<li><strong>Dopamine from midbrain (VTA/SNc → modulatory).</strong></li>
</ul>
<p><img src="/images/notes/Architecture of the Brain-20250816163012129.webp" style="width: 100%" class="center" alt="Architecture of the Brain-20250816163012129"></p>
<h4 id="pallidum-globus-pallidus">Pallidum (Globus Pallidus)<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#pallidum-globus-pallidus">#</a></h4>
<ul>
<li>Output stage of the basal ganglia (striatum zone).</li>
<li>Constantly inhibits thalamus (a “<strong>brake</strong>” on movement).
<ul>
<li>Sends inhibitory (GABAergic) output to the thalamus and brainstem</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>When striatum activates the direct pathway → it inhibits pallidum → brake lifted → thalamus excites cortex → action executed.</li>
<li>When the indirect pathway is dominant → pallidum inhibition persists → action suppressed.</li>
<li><strong>Role:</strong> The “final <em>gatekeeper</em>” controlling whether cortical action plans actually happen.</li>
</ul>
<p>Very close to the <strong>reinforcement learning loop</strong>, this is why the actor critic model of the brain makes sense.</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Cortex</strong> suggests possible actions based on goals and environment.</li>
<li><strong>Striatum</strong> receives these options and, influenced by <strong>dopamine from VTA</strong>, learns which are valuable.</li>
<li><strong>Pallidum</strong> implements the “brake system,” allowing only the favored action through.</li>
<li>The executed action leads to an outcome (reward or not).</li>
<li><strong>VTA</strong> compares outcome vs expectation → updates dopamine signals → reshapes striatal synapses → next time, the system is better tuned.</li>
</ol>
<p>So pallidum directly filters the correct action.
Both are inhibitory, but:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Pallidum</strong> = “which action should happen?” → targets motor thalamus for movement control.</li>
<li><strong>TRN</strong> = “which information should be noticed?” → targets sensory thalamus for attention and gating.</li>
</ul>

<h4 id="the-brain-stem">The brain stem<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#the-brain-stem">#</a></h4>
<p></p><figure class="center">
<img src="/images/notes/Architecture of the Brain-20250313215419034.webp" style="width: 100%" alt="Architecture of the Brain-20250313215419034" title="Architecture of the Brain-20250313215419034">

The brain stem is made of the medulla, Pons, and Midbrain.<p></p>
<p><strong>Medulla</strong></p>
<blockquote>
<p>The medulla, the most caudal portion of the brain stem, is a direct extension of the spinal cord and resembles the spinal cord both in organization and function. Neuronal groups in the medulla participate in regulating blood pressure and respiration. The medulla also contains neuronal groups that are early components of pathways that mediate taste, hearing, and maintenance of balance as well as the control of neck and facial muscles.</p>
</blockquote>
<p><strong>Pons</strong></p>
<blockquote>
<p>The pons lies rostral to the medulla and protrudes from the ventral surface of the brain stem. The ventral portion of the pons contains the pontine nuclei, groups of neurons that relay information about movement and sensation from the cerebral cortex to the cerebellum. The dorsal portion of the pons contains structures involved in respiration, taste, and sleep.</p>

<p><strong>Midbrain</strong></p>
<blockquote>
<p>The midbrain, the smallest part of the brain stem, lies rostral to the pons. Nuclei in the midbrain provide important linkages between components of the motor system, particularly the cerebellum, basal ganglia, and cerebral hemispheres [...] The midbrain also contains components of the auditory and visual systems. Finally, several regions of the midbrain give rise to pathways that are connected to the extraocular muscles that control eye movements.</p>
</blockquote>
<h4 id="the-thalamus">The Thalamus<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#the-thalamus">#</a></h4>
<p>This is one of the most complex brain areas. It has a role in consciousness, different firing patterns here if you are asleep or not. We can see this section as the <strong>routing board</strong> of the brain (almost every sense of the human brain passes through the thalamus, but not the sense of smell).</p>
<p>It receives information from sensory areas, but above all from cortical areas.
This is also why we think the <strong>thalamus</strong> plays an important role with regards to <em><strong>consciousness</strong></em>.</p>
<p>The entire cortex is connected to the thalamus, it also controls the communication between different brain regions, between one cortical area and the other cortical area. We have lots of back connections, probably because of attention selection and similar things.</p>
<p><img src="/images/notes/Architecture of the Brain-20250824211656280.webp" style="width: 100%" class="center" alt="Architecture of the Brain-20250824211656280"></p>
<blockquote>
<p>Thalamic DBS can rouse patients from minimally conscious state</p>

<h4 id="thalamic-reticular-nucleus">Thalamic Reticular Nucleus<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#thalamic-reticular-nucleus">#</a></h4>
<p>Here we have lots of GABAenergic neurons that project back to the thalamus, which is why it is believed to act as a gate.</p>
<p>The <strong>thalamic reticular nucleus</strong> acts as a gateway between thalamus and cortical connections, they are called <em>attention centers</em> (like amygdala) and modulate the thalamic information to the cortex, and most of the connections here are <em>inhibitory by nature</em>.</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Gatekeeper of thalamic relay:</strong>
<ul>
<li>TRN <strong>modulates the flow of sensory information</strong> from thalamus to cortex.</li>
<li>Can <strong>suppress or enhance</strong> signals depending on attention, state, or timing.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Control of attention and sensory filtering:</strong>
<ul>
<li>By selectively inhibiting thalamic neurons, TRN helps <strong>focus cortical processing on relevant stimuli</strong>.</li>
<li>Prevents irrelevant signals from reaching the cortex (“sensory gating”).</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Generating rhythmic activity:</strong>
<ul>
<li>TRN contributes to <strong>thalamocortical oscillations</strong>, e.g., <strong>sleep spindles</strong> during non-REM sleep.</li>
<li>These oscillations are important for <strong>memory consolidation and attentional control</strong>.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>
<h4 id="the-pulvinar">The Pulvinar<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#the-pulvinar">#</a></h4>
<p>We can view this as a second order thalamic connection.
The <strong>pulvinar</strong> is the <strong>largest nucleus of the thalamus</strong> in primates, sitting at the very back (posterior thalamus).</p>
<p>Unlike the <strong>lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN)</strong>, which gets input from the retina, the pulvinar gets most of its driving input from <strong>cortex</strong> (especially visual areas like V1, V2, MT, parietal cortex).</p>
<ul>
<li>Then, it sends projections back <strong>to multiple cortical visual areas</strong>.</li>
<li>It’s basically a “hub” for visual cortex communication.</li>
</ul>
<p>Researchers think of the pulvinar as a <strong>higher-order thalamic relay</strong> that:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Coordinates cortical areas</strong>: For example, making sure motion areas (MT) and shape/scene areas (V4, IT) talk to each other efficiently.</li>
<li><strong>Regulates attention</strong>: Helps filter out distractions and enhance relevant stimuli. If pulvinar is damaged, people have trouble ignoring irrelevant visual clutter.</li>
<li><strong>Synchronizes rhythms</strong>: Acts like a “metronome,” helping distant cortical regions stay in step during visual tasks.</li>
</ul>

<p>It sits near the enthorinal cortex (this sends much input into the hippocampus, mainly in two pathways)
Dentate Gyrus or dentate region -> CA3 -> CA1 (the output of the hippocampus, connected to the rest of the brain).</p>

<li>Entorhinal cortex has about 200k cells (10-20% interneurons)</li>
<li>Dentate Gyrus has about 1.2kk granule cells. (some sort of a sparse encoder, it is separating patterns)
<ul>
<li>4k basket cells</li>
<li>32k Hilar interneurons (20k mossy cells)</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>CA3/CA1 about 330k/420k piramidal cells.
<ul>
<li>CA3 has many auto-associative recurrent architectures, it is believed to be doing pattern completion after separation with a given context.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>The dentate gyrus has much more neurons than other neurons.</p>
<h4 id="random-functional-areas">Random Functional areas<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#random-functional-areas">#</a></h4>
<p>Mostly the maps to the functions of the brain has been developed by analysis of damages on these regions. A notable example is Phineas Gage's case on frontal cortex damage. He completely changed his personality. (valuation and planning abilities).</p>
<p>Broca's area is used for <strong>speech production</strong>: they can understand sentences but could not produce words.</p>
<p><strong>Wernicke's area</strong>: cannot interpret speeches, they can produce sentences, but they are irrelevant.</p>
<h4 id="entorhynal-cortex">Entorhynal Cortex<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#entorhynal-cortex">#</a></h4>
<p>The researchers performed extensive patch-clamp recordings from over 600 pairs of stellate cells (the primary cell type in MEC layer II that likely includes <strong>grid cells</strong>, we study those in <a href="/notes/memory-in-human-brain/">Memory in Human Brain</a>) in rat brain slices.
The main neurons there are <strong>stellate cells</strong>. Researchers wanted to know: <em>How do these stellate cells connect to each other?</em></p>
<p>Their main findings were:</p>
<p><strong>1. Absence of Direct Excitation:</strong> Despite recording from 644 stellate cell pairs, they found virtually no direct excitatory connections between these cells in mature tissue. Only 4% showed any inhibitory responses to single-cell stimulation.</p>
<p><strong>2. Inhibitory Network Structure:</strong> When multiple stellate cells were stimulated simultaneously, 64% of target cells showed inhibitory responses. These were mediated by fast-spiking interneurons rather than direct connections.</p>
<p><strong>3. Developmental Changes:</strong> Young animals (postnatal days 10-15) showed rare excitatory connections between stellate cells that disappeared by 4 weeks of age, while inhibitory connectivity matured over the same period.</p>
<p><strong>4. Confirmation with Optogenetics:</strong> Using light-activated channelrhodopsin to stimulate populations of stellate cells confirmed that connectivity was predominantly inhibitory.</p>

Identified by Epstein &#x26; Kanwisher in 1998 using fMRI. They noticed a region that lights up strongly when subjects look at pictures of places (houses, landscapes, city streets) compared to faces, objects, or scrambled images.</p>
<h4 id="extrastriate-body-area">Extrastriate Body Area<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#extrastriate-body-area">#</a></h4>
<p><strong>Extrastriate body area</strong>: responds to body parts (no faces, no random cartoons, yes stickman).</p>
<p>There are other neurons that activate for specific parts of the brain.</p>
<h4 id="face-neurons">Face Neurons<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#face-neurons">#</a></h4>
<p>Bruce Desimone and Gross in 1981 identified areas of the brain that activate by faces</p>
<h3 id="visual-areas">Visual Areas<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#visual-areas">#</a></h3>
<h4 id="hierarchical-organization-of-visual-processing">Hierarchical organization of visual processing<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#hierarchical-organization-of-visual-processing">#</a></h4>
<p>This has been the main inspiration for visual processing architectures like <a href="/notes/convolutional-neural-network/">Convolutional Neural Network</a>. They start to recognize simple features, and then compose them in more complex shapes. This has been also validated with mechanistic interpretability techniques, and it is overall quite fascinating.</p>
<p>In visual things there are V1, V2, V4 TEO, and cortex areas, in quite organized parts. Mainly in the inferior temporal lobe, which is the "what" pathway for object recognition.</p>
<h4 id="bidirectional-connections">Bidirectional Connections<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#bidirectional-connections">#</a></h4>
<p>Convolutional nets are not RNN, they do not use higher contextual information to <strong>inform back the small parts</strong>. Context is important for humans. Here information is <strong>bidirectional</strong>, this is the main difference for these biological connections compared to networks like <a href="/notes/convolutional-neural-network/">Convolutional Neural Network</a>.
The hypothesis is that it helps to interpret what is seen, some feedback signal, somehow makes me think about <a href="https://psycnet.apa.org/record/2007-01197-000">(Hofstadter 2007)</a>.
This gives grounds to explain some of the visual illusions.</p>
<p>One hypothesis we have is that it <strong>enlarges</strong> the receptive field, and uses <em>attention</em> on the part that could be more interesting.</p>
<p>Main roles:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Attention &#x26; prediction:</strong> higher areas tell earlier areas <em>what to look for</em> (“expect a face here”).</li>
<li><strong>Context filling:</strong> feedback helps sharpen perception (e.g., blurry letters become readable in context).</li>
</ul>
<h4 id="visual-information-pathways">Visual information pathways<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#visual-information-pathways">#</a></h4>
<p>In visual processing, there are two streams of connections that give different information on</p>
<ul>
<li>Where (<strong>Dorsal</strong>, vestibular information about body positions (eye, arm etc...), that help in action <em>planning</em>)
<ul>
<li>They recognize the objects, but not able to know spatial position between them.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>What (<strong>Ventral</strong>, more in object recognition)
<ul>
<li>People that have damage here are not able to recognize the objects, but just know that they exists.</li>
<li>But they would be able to manipulate the object, not consciously being able to recognize it.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>They share the first layers (v1,v2) for low level details.
They tested this in object discrimination and landmark discrimination tasks with dorsal and ventral damages.</li>
</ul>
<p>We also study the role of learning, since many learning activities pass through the vision part, that is articulated in <a href="/notes/memory-in-human-brain/">Memory in Human Brain</a>.
If you want a better understanding of vision in general see <a href="/notes/human-vision/">Human Vision</a>.</p>
<h4 id="dorsal-stream">Dorsal stream<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#dorsal-stream">#</a></h4>
<p>Looking at the visual stream, we observe that the organization of the parts is not casual, the information gets into the somatosensory area. Posterior parietal cortex uses the information to build model of yourself (egocentric maps), and it is quite close to the cortices, close to the <strong>planning part</strong> of the brain.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Premotor</strong> area is for <em>physical intention</em> formation

</ul>
<h4 id="ventral-stream">Ventral Stream<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#ventral-stream">#</a></h4>
<p>The other stream is close to the broca's area for speech processing and speech interpretation, to associate these two streams.
Visual area is heavily linked to the auditory one, but not much with the speech planning area, this explains also why we have these similitudes.</p>

<h4 id="representation-of-intentions">Representation of Intentions<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#representation-of-intentions">#</a></h4>
<p>Anterior regions represent goals and intentions because they need <strong>abstract, context-dependent, temporally extended information</strong> that guides posterior motor circuits for execution.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Premotor and motor cortex (posterior frontal regions):</strong>
<ul>
<li>Represent <strong>specific movements</strong>: which muscles contract, sequence of joint actions.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>More anterior regions (prefrontal cortex, especially dorsolateral PFC):</strong>
<ul>
<li>Represent <strong>higher-level plans, goals, and intentions</strong>.</li>
<li>Not tied to a particular movement; more about <em>what you want to achieve</em>.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
