28:T38055,<h2 id="zero-knowledge-proofs">Zero Knowledge Proofs<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#zero-knowledge-proofs">#</a></h2>
<h3 id="motivation--foundational-context">Motivation &#x26; Foundational Context<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#motivation--foundational-context">#</a></h3>
<p>ZKPs sit at the intersection of Complexity Theory, Cryptography, and Interactive Proof Systems. The core question: <em>can you convince someone you know something without revealing what you know?</em> This has profound implications for privacy-preserving authentication, blockchain protocols, and verifiable computation.</p>
<h3 id="interactive-proof-systems">Interactive Proof Systems<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#interactive-proof-systems">#</a></h3>
<p><a href="/notes/interactive-theorem-provers/">Interactive Theorem Provers</a></p>
<h4 id="the-classical-model">The Classical Model<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#the-classical-model">#</a></h4>
<p>An <strong>interactive proof system</strong> involves two parties exchanging messages:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Prover (P)</strong>: Computationally unbounded; claims a statement is true.</li>
<li><strong>Verifier (V)</strong>: Probabilistic polynomial-time (PPT); decides accept/reject.</li>
</ul>
<blockquote>
<p>"An interactive proof system for a language <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">L</span></span></span></span> is a protocol <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.1389em;">P</span><span class="mpunct">,</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.2222em;">V</span><span class="mclose">)</span></span></span></span> such that completeness and soundness hold." ~ <em>Goldwasser, Micali, Rackoff (1985)</em></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The class of languages with interactive proofs is <strong>IP</strong>, and famously <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord text"><span class="mord">IP</span></span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">=</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord text"><span class="mord">PSPACE</span></span></span></span></span>.</p>
<h4 id="completeness-and-soundness">Completeness and Soundness<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#completeness-and-soundness">#</a></h4>
<p>These are the two non-negotiable properties of any proof system:</p>
<blockquote>
<p><strong>Completeness</strong>: If <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" <blockquote>

</blockquote>
<p>The <strong>soundness error</strong> <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">ϵ</span></span></span></span> is the maximum cheating probability; for practical systems, we require <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.7719em;vertical-align:-0.136em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">ϵ</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">≤</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.8491em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord">2</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.8491em;"><span style="top:-3.063em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mord mtight"><span class="mord mtight">−</span><span class="mord mathnormal mtight">λ</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span>.</p>
<h3 id="zero-knowledge-the-core-definition">Zero Knowledge: The Core Definition<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#zero-knowledge-the-core-definition">#</a></h3>
<h4 id="intuition">Intuition<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#intuition">#</a></h4>
<p>The verifier learns <strong>nothing</strong> beyond the truth of the statement. Formally, whatever the verifier sees during the interaction could have been produced <em>without the prover</em> — using only knowledge that the statement is true.</p>
<p>The canonical example: proving knowledge of a 3-coloring of a graph without revealing the coloring.</p>
<h4 id="the-simulator-paradigm">The Simulator Paradigm<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#the-simulator-paradigm">#</a></h4>
<p>Zero knowledge is defined via <strong>simulation</strong>: a protocol is ZK if there exists a PPT simulator <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathcal" style="margin-right:0.075em;">S</span></span></span></span> such that the simulated transcript is computationally indistinguishable from the real transcript.</p>
<blockquote>
<p><strong>Zero Knowledge Property</strong>: <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord">∀</span></span></span></span> PPT verifier <span class="katex-error" title="ParseError: KaTeX parse error: Expected group after &#x27;^&#x27; at position 2: V^̲_" style="color:#cc0000">V^_</span>, <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord">∃</span></span></span></span> PPT simulator <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathcal" style="margin-right:0.075em;">S</span></span></span></span> such that: <span class="katex-error" title="ParseError: KaTeX parse error: Expected group after &#x27;^&#x27; at position 16: {\text{View}_{V^̲_}[\langle P(w)…" style="color:#cc0000">{\text{View}_{V^_}[\langle P(w), V^*(z) \rangle(x)]}_{x \in L, w, z} \approx_c {\mathcal{S}(x, z)}_{x \in L, z}</span></p>
</blockquote>
<p>where <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0269em;">w</span></span></span></span> is the witness, <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.044em;">z</span></span></span></span> is <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6887em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.2222em;">V</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.6887em;"><span style="top:-3.063em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mbin mtight">∗</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span>'s auxiliary input, and <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6331em;vertical-align:-0.15em;"></span><span class="mrel"><span class="mrel">≈</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t vlist-t2"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.1514em;"><span style="top:-2.55em;margin-left:0em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mord mathnormal mtight">c</span></span></span></span><span class="vlist-s">​</span></span><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.15em;"><span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span> denotes computational indistinguishability.</p>
<h4 id="three-flavors-of-zero-knowledge">Three Flavors of Zero Knowledge<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#three-flavors-of-zero-knowledge">#</a></h4>

























<table><thead><tr><th align="left">Variant</th><th align="left">Indistinguishability</th><th align="left">Strength</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td align="left"><strong>Perfect ZK</strong></td><td align="left">Identical distributions</td><td align="left">Strongest; information-theoretic</td></tr><tr><td align="left"><strong>Statistical ZK (SZK)</strong></td><td align="left">Statistically close (<span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.7719em;vertical-align:-0.136em;"></span><span class="mrel">≤</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.8889em;vertical-align:-0.1944em;"></span><span class="mord text"><span class="mord">negl</span></span></span></span></span>)</td><td align="left">Very strong; holds vs. unbounded adversary</td></tr><tr><td align="left"><strong>Computational ZK (CZK)</strong></td><td align="left">Computationally indistinguishable</td><td align="left">Standard; relies on hardness assumptions</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>For most practical constructions (SNARKs, STARKs), <strong>computational ZK</strong> suffices.</p>
<h3 id="sigma-protocols-σ-protocols">Sigma Protocols (Σ-Protocols)<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#sigma-protocols-σ-protocols">#</a></h3>

<p>Sigma protocols are <strong>3-move</strong> public-coin interactive proofs:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Commit</strong> (<span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">a</span></span></span></span>): Prover sends commitment <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">a</span></span></span></span>.</li>
<li><strong>Challenge</strong> (<span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">e</span></span></span></span>): Verifier sends random challenge <span class="katex-error" title="ParseError: KaTeX parse error: Unexpected end of input in a macro argument, expected &#x27;}&#x27; at end of input: e \xleftarrow{" style="color:#cc0000">e \xleftarrow{</span>} {0,1}^\lambda$.</li>
<li><strong>Response</strong> (<span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.044em;">z</span></span></span></span>): Prover sends response <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.044em;">z</span></span></span></span>.</li>

<p>Verifier accepts/rejects based on <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal">a</span><span class="mpunct">,</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">e</span><span class="mpunct">,</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.044em;">z</span><span class="mclose">)</span></span></span></span>.</p>

<h4 id="fiat-shamir-transform">Fiat-Shamir Transform<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#fiat-shamir-transform">#</a></h4>
<p>Converts an interactive Sigma protocol into a <strong>non-interactive ZK (NIZK)</strong> proof in the Random Oracle Model:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Replace the verifier's random challenge with a hash: <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">e</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">=</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0813em;">H</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal">x</span><span class="mord">∣</span><span class="mord mathnormal">a</span><span class="mclose">)</span></span></span></span>.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The resulting proof <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0359em;">π</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">=</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal">a</span><span class="mpunct">,</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.044em;">z</span><span class="mclose">)</span></span></span></span> is a <strong>signature of knowledge</strong>. Security relies on modeling <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0813em;">H</span></span></span></span> as a random oracle — in the standard model this is much harder to achieve (see Canetti-Goldreich-Halevi impossibility).</p>
<p>This single trick is what converts the interactive Schnorr protocol into a non-interactive proof (and the Schnorr signature scheme), and is the basis for making all modern SNARK/STARK systems non-interactive.</p>
<h3 id="non-interactive-zero-knowledge-nizk">Non-Interactive Zero Knowledge (NIZK)<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#non-interactive-zero-knowledge-nizk">#</a></h3>
<h4 id="definition">Definition<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#definition">#</a></h4>
<p>A NIZK proof system has:</p>

<h3 id="snarks">SNARKs<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#snarks">#</a></h3>
<h4 id="definition-1">Definition<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#definition-1">#</a></h4>
<p><strong>Succinct Non-interactive ARgument of Knowledge (SNARK)</strong>:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Succinct</strong>: Proof size <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord">∣</span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0359em;">π</span><span class="mord">∣</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">=</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">O</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord text"><span class="mord">poly</span></span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal">λ</span><span class="mclose">))</span></span></span></span>, independent of witness size; verification in <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">O</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord text"><span class="mord">poly</span></span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal">λ</span><span class="mclose">)</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2222em;"></span><span class="mbin">⋅</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2222em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord">∣</span><span class="mord mathnormal">x</span><span class="mord">∣</span><span class="mclose">)</span></span></span></span>.</li>
<li><strong>Non-interactive</strong>: Single message from prover to verifier.</li>
<li><strong>Argument</strong>: Soundness holds only against computationally bounded provers (not information-theoretic).</li>
<li><strong>of Knowledge</strong>: Extractability — a prover producing a valid proof must "know" a witness (formalized via a knowledge extractor).</li>
</ul>
<blockquote>
<p><strong>Knowledge Soundness</strong>: <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord">∀</span></span></span></span> PPT <span class="katex-error" title="ParseError: KaTeX parse error: Expected group after &#x27;^&#x27; at position 2: P^̲_" style="color:#cc0000">P^_</span>, <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord">∃</span></span></span></span> PPT extractor <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathcal" style="margin-right:0.0894em;">E</span></span></span></span> s.t.: <span class="katex-error" title="ParseError: KaTeX parse error: Expected group after &#x27;^&#x27; at position 96: …x) \leftarrow P^̲_(\sigma); w \l…" style="color:#cc0000">\Pr\left[\text{Verify}(\sigma, x, \pi) = 1 \wedge (x, w) \notin R ;\Big|; (\pi, x) \leftarrow P^_(\sigma); w \leftarrow \mathcal{E}^{P^*}(\sigma)\right] \leq \text{negl}(\lambda)</span></p>
</blockquote>
<h4 id="zk-snarks-in-practice">zk-SNARKs in Practice<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#zk-snarks-in-practice">#</a></h4>
<p>The dominant paradigm: <strong>arithmetic circuit satisfiability</strong>. Given a circuit <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0715em;">C</span></span></span></span> over field <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6889em;"></span><span class="mord mathbb">F</span></span></span></span>, prove <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord">∃</span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0269em;">w</span></span></span></span> s.t. <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0715em;">C</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal">x</span><span class="mpunct">,</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0269em;">w</span><span class="mclose">)</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">=</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6444em;"></span><span class="mord">0</span></span></span></span>.</p>
<p>Key pipeline:</p>
<ol>

<h3 id="starks">STARKs<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#starks">#</a></h3>
<h4 id="design-principles">Design Principles<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#design-principles">#</a></h4>
<p><strong>Scalable Transparent ARguments of Knowledge</strong>:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Transparent</strong>: No trusted setup; public randomness only (eliminates toxic waste problem).</li>
<li><strong>Scalable</strong>: Prover time <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">O</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal">n</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mop">lo<span style="margin-right:0.0139em;">g</span></span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">n</span><span class="mclose">)</span></span></span></span>, but proof size <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1.1484em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">O</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mop"><span class="mop">lo<span style="margin-right:0.0139em;">g</span></span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.8984em;"><span style="top:-3.1473em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mord mtight">2</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">n</span><span class="mclose">)</span></span></span></span> — larger than SNARKs but no CRS.</li>
<li><strong>Post-quantum</strong>: Security based on collision-resistant hash functions, not elliptic curves.</li>
</ul>
<p>Key building blocks:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>FRI Protocol</strong> (Fast Reed-Solomon IOP of Proximity): Proves a committed function is close to a low-degree polynomial. See Reed-Solomon Codes.</li>
<li><strong>Algebraic Intermediate Representation (AIR)</strong>: Constraint system using execution trace + transition constraints.</li>
</ul>

<h3 id="blockchain-applications-detailed">Blockchain Applications (Detailed)<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#blockchain-applications-detailed">#</a></h3>
<h4 id="zk-rollups-l2-scaling">ZK-Rollups (L2 Scaling)<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#zk-rollups-l2-scaling">#</a></h4>
<p>zkRollups, Layer 2 Scaling</p>
<p>ZK-rollups are the dominant blockchain application of ZKPs. They execute transactions off-chain, generate a validity proof (SNARK or STARK), and post the proof + compressed state diff to L1 (typically Ethereum). The L1 contract verifies the proof — if it passes, the entire batch is final.</p>
<p>The key value proposition: <strong>batch thousands of transactions into one proof</strong>, reducing per-transaction gas cost by ~90%. As of 2025, ZK-based rollups hold >$28B in Total Value Locked.</p>
<p><strong>How it works concretely</strong>:</p>
<ol>
<li>Users submit transactions to L2 sequencer (same UX as Ethereum).</li>
<li>Sequencer executes transactions, computes new state root.</li>
<li>Prover generates a ZK proof that the state transition is valid.</li>
<li>Proof + compressed state diff posted to L1 Ethereum contract.</li>
<li>L1 verifier contract checks the proof → if valid, state is finalized.</li>
</ol>
<p><strong>Major projects</strong>: zkSync Era (SNARK-based, zkEVM), StarkNet (STARK-based, Cairo language), Polygon zkEVM, Scroll, Linea (ConsenSys).</p>
<h4 id="zk-rollups-vs-optimistic-rollups">ZK-Rollups vs. Optimistic Rollups<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#zk-rollups-vs-optimistic-rollups">#</a></h4>
<p>Optimistic Rollups, Arbitrum, Optimism</p>
<p>Optimistic rollups are the alternative L2 scaling approach. They use <strong>fraud proofs</strong> instead of validity proofs — the fundamental difference is the proof model:</p>


















































<table><thead><tr><th align="left">Property</th><th align="left">ZK-Rollups</th><th align="left">Optimistic Rollups</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td align="left"><strong>Proof model</strong></td><td align="left">Validity proof (math: prove batch is correct <em>before</em> posting)</td><td align="left">Fraud proof (assume valid, challenge within 7-day window)</td></tr><tr><td align="left"><strong>Finality</strong></td><td align="left">Minutes (once proof verified on L1)</td><td align="left">~7 days (challenge window must close)</td></tr><tr><td align="left"><strong>Withdrawal to L1</strong></td><td align="left">Fast (proof settles immediately)</td><td align="left">7-day delay (or use liquidity bridges at a fee)</td></tr><tr><td align="left"><strong>Security assumption</strong></td><td align="left">Cryptographic (math)</td><td align="left">Game-theoretic (at least 1 honest watcher must exist)</td></tr><tr><td align="left"><strong>EVM compatibility</strong></td><td align="left">Improving (zkEVMs are maturing)</td><td align="left">Near-native (deploy existing Solidity with zero changes)</td></tr><tr><td align="left"><strong>Data posted to L1</strong></td><td align="left">Proof + compressed state diff</td><td align="left">Full transaction data (calldata/blobs)</td></tr><tr><td align="left"><strong>Prover cost</strong></td><td align="left">High (proof generation needs specialized hardware)</td><td align="left">Low (just re-execute and post)</td></tr><tr><td align="left"><strong>TVL (2025)</strong></td><td align="left">~$28B across ZK L2s</td><td align="left">Higher overall (Arbitrum, Optimism, Base)</td></tr></tbody></table>
<blockquote>
<p><strong>Optimistic rollup fraud proof mechanism</strong>: A sequencer posts batches to L1 asserting validity. Anyone can re-execute the batch and, if they find a discrepancy, submit a fraud proof. Two designs exist: <strong>single-round</strong> (Optimism — re-execute entire batch on L1) and <strong>interactive bisection</strong> (Arbitrum — narrow dispute to a single instruction via a binary search game, much more gas-efficient). If fraud is confirmed, the sequencer is slashed (loses staked collateral) and the batch is reverted.</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>The fraud proof mechanism on Arbitrum and Optimism has never been successfully exploited in production, but the security model is weaker than ZK: it relies on incentive-compatible game theory rather than mathematical proof. A successful DoS attack on challengers during the dispute window could, in theory, allow invalid state to be finalized.</p>
</blockquote>
<p><strong>Vitalik Buterin's view</strong>: ZK-rollups are likely to outperform long-term once zkEVM technology matures, but optimistic rollups dominate today due to easier developer migration and EVM compatibility.</p>
<h4 id="private-transactions">Private Transactions<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#private-transactions">#</a></h4>
<p>Zcash, Aztec Network, Tornado Cash</p>
<p>The original blockchain ZKP use case: prove a transaction is valid without revealing sender, receiver, or amount.</p>
<p><strong>Zcash</strong>: Each shielded transaction includes a Groth16 zk-SNARK proving that inputs equal outputs (conservation of value), the sender owns the input notes (knowledge of spending key), and no double-spend occurred (nullifier not in the spent set) — all without exposing any on-chain details.</p>
<p><strong>Aztec Network</strong>: Extends private transactions to full <strong>private smart contracts</strong> on Ethereum. Uses a dual-layer architecture: one layer encrypts transactions for privacy, a second layer compresses them for scalability (PLONK-based). Aztec developed Noir, a domain-specific language for writing ZK circuits, significantly lowering the barrier for developers.</p>
<p>Tradeoffs: proof generation is slow on consumer devices (seconds for Zcash, improving); regulatory friction in some jurisdictions; anonymity set size limits effective privacy (small anonymity sets leak information via traffic analysis).</p>
<h4 id="decentralized-identity-and-zk-kyc">Decentralized Identity and ZK-KYC<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#decentralized-identity-and-zk-kyc">#</a></h4>
<p>Verifiable Credentials, Polygon ID, Humanity Protocol, World ID</p>
<p>ZK-based identity enables proving attributes (age, citizenship, accreditation, KYC status) without revealing raw PII:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Prove you're over 18</strong> without revealing your birthdate.</li>
<li><strong>Prove you're KYC-verified</strong> without sharing identity documents.</li>
<li><strong>Prove you're a unique human</strong> (Sybil resistance) without biometric exposure.</li>
</ul>
<p>The credential model: a trusted issuer signs an attribute; the user holds the credential; a verifier checks a ZK proof derived from the credential. The proof reveals only the predicate (e.g., "age ≥ 18"), not the underlying data.</p>
<p><strong>Major projects</strong>: Humanity Protocol (palm biometrics → ZK proofs for proof-of-personhood), World ID / Worldcoin (iris scan → ZK proof of uniqueness), Polygon ID (verifiable credentials on Polygon), Privado ID (Deutsche Bank PoC).</p>
<p>This is GDPR-aligned by design: the data never leaves the user's device. The ZK-KYC market is projected to grow at ~40% CAGR.</p>
<h4 id="proof-of-reserves">Proof of Reserves<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#proof-of-reserves">#</a></h4>
<p>Proof of Solvency, <a href="/notes/merkle-trees/">Merkle Trees</a></p>
<p>Post-FTX, proof of reserves became critical infrastructure. A ZK proof can show that an exchange's total assets <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.7719em;vertical-align:-0.136em;"></span><span class="mrel">≥</span></span></span></span> total liabilities (sum of all customer balances) without revealing any individual customer's position.</p>
<p>The construction typically uses a Merkle sum tree of customer balances, where the exchange proves:</p>
<ol>
<li>Each leaf (customer balance) is non-negative.</li>
<li>The root sum equals the claimed total liability.</li>
<li>The exchange controls on-chain addresses with assets <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.7719em;vertical-align:-0.136em;"></span><span class="mrel">≥</span></span></span></span> the root sum.</li>
</ol>
<p>All via ZK proofs — no individual balance is ever revealed. OKX publishes monthly ZK-based PoR attestations.</p>
<p><strong>Limitation</strong>: Proves assets <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.7719em;vertical-align:-0.136em;"></span><span class="mrel">≥</span></span></span></span> known liabilities at a snapshot in time. Cannot prove absence of hidden off-balance-sheet liabilities without additional auditing.</p>
<h4 id="verifiable-computation-and-zkvms">Verifiable Computation and zkVMs<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#verifiable-computation-and-zkvms">#</a></h4>
<p>RISC Zero, SP1, zkVM</p>
<p>A <strong>zkVM</strong> (zero-knowledge virtual machine) generalises the SNARK circuit approach: instead of compiling each program into a bespoke circuit, you prove correct execution of a general-purpose instruction set (e.g., RISC-V).</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Write normal code (Rust, C) → execute off-chain → produce ZK proof that execution was correct → any on-chain verifier checks the proof without re-executing.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>This is the "any program is provable" endpoint of ZKP technology. The SNARK circuit example above (<span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.8974em;vertical-align:-0.0833em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathnormal">x</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.8141em;"><span style="top:-3.063em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mord mtight">2</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2222em;"></span><span class="mbin">+</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2222em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6667em;vertical-align:-0.0833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">x</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2222em;"></span><span class="mbin">+</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2222em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6444em;"></span><span class="mord">5</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">=</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6444em;"></span><span class="mord">11</span></span></span></span>) required manually flattening into gates; a zkVM does this automatically for arbitrary programs.</p>
<p><strong>Major projects</strong>: RISC Zero (RISC-V + STARKs), SP1 (Succinct), Valida.</p>
<p><strong>Tradeoffs</strong>: proof generation is 100–1000× slower than native execution; memory-intensive programs are hard to prove (the circuit size for the VM itself is large); tooling is improving rapidly.</p>
<h4 id="zk-bridges-and-light-clients">ZK Bridges and Light Clients<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#zk-bridges-and-light-clients">#</a></h4>
<p>Cross-Chain Bridges, ZeroSync</p>
<p>Traditional cross-chain bridges rely on multisig committees — if the committee is compromised, funds are stolen (e.g., Ronin bridge: <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.8889em;vertical-align:-0.1944em;"></span><span class="mord">625</span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.109em;">M</span><span class="mpunct">,</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.1389em;">W</span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">or</span><span class="mord mathnormal">mh</span><span class="mord mathnormal">o</span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0197em;">l</span><span class="mord mathnormal">e</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">:</span></span></span></span>320M). ZK bridges replace the multisig with a cryptographic proof: a SNARK proves that <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.109em;">N</span></span></span></span> validators signed the source chain's block header, allowing the destination chain to verify trustlessly.</p>
<p><strong>ZK light clients</strong>: Instead of downloading and validating every block header, a light client verifies a single ZK proof that the entire chain history (or a segment) is valid. ZeroSync is building this for Bitcoin — proving the Bitcoin state transition function via STARKs.</p>
<p><strong>Tradeoffs</strong>: proving consensus (e.g., BLS signature verification) inside a ZK circuit is computationally expensive; latency from proof generation; still largely in research/prototype stage.</p>
<h4 id="zk--machine-learning-emerging">ZK + Machine Learning (Emerging)<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#zk--machine-learning-emerging">#</a></h4>
<p>Verifiable AI, EZKL, Modulus Labs</p>
<p>An emerging frontier: using ZK proofs to verify ML model inference or training correctness without revealing the model weights or training data. This enables <strong>verifiable AI</strong> — critical if AI agents are making financial decisions on-chain.</p>
<p>The construction: encode a neural network's forward pass as an arithmetic circuit, prove that given input <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">x</span></span></span></span>, the model produces output <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.625em;vertical-align:-0.1944em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0359em;">y</span></span></span></span>. The proof attests to correct execution of the specific model without revealing its parameters.</p>
<p><strong>Research projects</strong>: EZKL (proves ML inference), Modulus Labs, zkFDL (federated learning).</p>
<p><strong>Tradeoffs</strong>: proving even a small neural network is extremely expensive — the circuit size for a single inference of a modest model can be enormous. This is the primary bottleneck. Current approaches use quantisation and circuit-friendly architectures to reduce overhead, but it remains very early stage.</p>
<blockquote>
<p><strong>Connection to multi-agent AI safety</strong>: if AI agents operate autonomously on-chain (e.g., managing DeFi positions, participating in governance), ZK proofs could serve as the verification layer ensuring agents are running the claimed model faithfully. This creates a mechanism design problem: how to structure verification incentives so that agent behavior is auditable without revealing proprietary model details. See Mechanism Design, Multi-Agent Systems.</p>
</blockquote>
<h3 id="critical-pitfalls--warnings">Critical Pitfalls &#x26; Warnings<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#critical-pitfalls--warnings">#</a></h3>
<h4 id="trusted-setup-failures">Trusted Setup Failures<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#trusted-setup-failures">#</a></h4>
<blockquote>
<p>If the toxic waste <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.1132em;">τ</span></span></span></span> from a SNARK CRS ceremony is not destroyed, an adversary can forge arbitrary proofs.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Mitigations:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>MPC ceremonies</strong> (Powers of Tau): <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">n</span></span></span></span>-of-<span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">n</span></span></span></span> threshold — safe if <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.7719em;vertical-align:-0.136em;"></span><span class="mrel">≥</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6444em;"></span><span class="mord">1</span></span></span></span> participant is honest.</li>
<li><strong>Universal setups</strong> (PLONK): Reuse same SRS across circuits.</li>
<li><strong>Transparent setups</strong> (STARKs): Eliminate the problem entirely.</li>
</ul>
<h4 id="soundness-vs-knowledge-soundness">Soundness vs. Knowledge Soundness<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#soundness-vs-knowledge-soundness">#</a></h4>
<ul>
<li><strong>Soundness</strong>: No PPT prover can convince verifier of false statement.</li>
<li><strong>Knowledge soundness</strong>: Stronger — any convincing prover must know a witness.</li>
<li>Many protocols achieve soundness but NOT knowledge soundness. For authentication and credential systems, you need the latter.</li>
</ul>
<h4 id="non-black-box-vs-black-box-simulation">Non-Black-Box vs. Black-Box Simulation<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#non-black-box-vs-black-box-simulation">#</a></h4>
<p>Standard ZK definitions use black-box simulation (simulator can rewind <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6887em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.2222em;">V</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.6887em;"><span style="top:-3.063em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mbin mtight">∗</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span>). Non-black-box simulation (Barak 2001) achieves round-efficient ZK for NP but at high computational cost. Practical systems almost always use black-box.</p>
<h4 id="completenesssoundnesszk-the-tension">Completeness–Soundness–ZK: The Tension<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#completenesssoundnesszk-the-tension">#</a></h4>
<blockquote>
<p>You cannot simultaneously achieve perfect completeness, perfect soundness, and perfect ZK (unless <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord text"><span class="mord">BPP</span></span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">=</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord text"><span class="mord">IP</span></span></span></span></span>).</p>
</blockquote>
<ul>
<li>Perfect ZK + negligible soundness error: feasible.</li>
<li>Statistical ZK (SZK) is a rich class but collapses to AM ∩ coAM under plausible assumptions.</li>
</ul>
