<h4 id="rate-code-vs-temporal-code">Rate code vs Temporal Code<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#rate-code-vs-temporal-code">#</a></h4>
<p><strong>Rate-code:</strong> The <strong>information</strong> a neuron transmits is encoded in the <strong>average firing rate</strong>, which means how many spikes it produces in a given time window.
<strong>Temporal-code</strong>: The <strong>precise timing</strong> of spikes, down to millisecond precision, <strong>carries information</strong>, not just the firing rate. The specific activity pattern in time carries information.</p>
<p>Cortical areas are usually outer encoded, meaning a population of neurons encode features, while higher and deeper level neurons are usually linear, and encode simple features.
One example are place neurons studied in <a href="/notes/memory-in-human-brain/">Memory in Human Brain</a></p>

<h4 id="miniature-end-plate-potentials">Miniature End Plate Potentials<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#miniature-end-plate-potentials">#</a></h4>
<p><strong>Miniature end-plate potentials (MEPPs)</strong> are the <strong>tiny depolarizations</strong> recorded at the muscle end plate <strong>in the absence of nerve stimulation</strong>, of about 1mV (while normal EPP are of 40-50 mV).</p>
<p>They are the "background noise" of neurotransmission, but in fact they reveal something very fundamental: that <strong>acetylcholine (ACh) is released in discrete packets (quanta)</strong>, even without action potentials.</p>
<p>Summed together, many vesicles released simultaneously during a real presynaptic action potential → a full EPP → muscle contraction.</p>
<p>This was an important experiment to prove the <strong>quantization of the vesicles</strong> in 1950</p>
