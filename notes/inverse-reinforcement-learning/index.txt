31:T3a9c2,<h2 id="bayesian-inverse-reinforcement-learning-birl">Bayesian Inverse Reinforcement Learning (BIRL)<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#bayesian-inverse-reinforcement-learning-birl">#</a></h2>
<h3 id="background-the-irl-problem-setup">Background: The IRL Problem Setup<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#background-the-irl-problem-setup">#</a></h3>
<p>Inverse Reinforcement Learning sits at the intersection of Reinforcement Learning, Bayesian Inference, and Imitation Learning. The core question is: <em>given observed expert behavior, what reward function is the expert optimizing?</em> This is strictly harder than forward RL, and the key challenge is that the problem is fundamentally <strong>ill-posed</strong>.</p>
<h4 id="the-irl-problem-definition">The IRL Problem Definition<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#the-irl-problem-definition">#</a></h4>
<blockquote>

<h4 id="the-ill-posedness-problem">The Ill-Posedness Problem<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#the-ill-posedness-problem">#</a></h4>
<blockquote>
<p>IRL is fundamentally ill-posed: many distinct reward functions explain the same observed behavior, including the degenerate <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0077em;">R</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">≡</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6444em;"></span><span class="mord">0</span></span></span></span>.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The three classical approaches to resolving this ambiguity are:</p>

<p>The focus of these notes is the <strong>Bayesian</strong> approach. The key motivation: MAP and MLE methods collapse all probability mass on a single reward function, which is brittle when data is scarce. BIRL maintains a full distribution over <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0077em;">R</span></span></span></span>.</p>

<h3 id="inference-mcmc-methods">Inference: MCMC Methods<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#inference-mcmc-methods">#</a></h3>
<h4 id="the-computational-challenge">The Computational Challenge<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#the-computational-challenge">#</a></h4>
<p>The posterior <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">p</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0077em;">R</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">∣</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord mathcal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">D</span><span class="mclose">)</span></span></span></span> has two sources of intractability:</p>
<ol>
<li>The normalizing constant <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0715em;">Z</span></span></span></span> is an integral over a continuous (often high-dimensional) reward space</li>

<h4 id="valuewalk-and-modern-extensions">ValueWalk and Modern Extensions<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#valuewalk-and-modern-extensions">#</a></h4>
<p><strong>ValueWalk</strong> (Bajgar et al., 2024) addresses PolicyWalk's scalability by proposing in the <strong>space of Q-functions</strong> rather than directly in reward space:</p>
<ul>
<li>Observation: <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.8831em;vertical-align:-0.1944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathnormal">Q</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.6887em;"><span style="top:-3.063em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mbin mtight">∗</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span> is a sufficient statistic for policy computation; the reward can be read back from <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.8831em;vertical-align:-0.1944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathnormal">Q</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.6887em;"><span style="top:-3.063em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mbin mtight">∗</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span> via the Bellman equations</li>
<li>Sampling Q-functions avoids solving the full forward problem at each step</li>
<li>Allows MCMC-based BIRL to scale to continuous state spaces</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>AVRIL</strong> (Chan &#x26; van der Schaar, 2021) takes a variational inference approach:</p>
<ul>
<li>Treats the reward as a <strong>latent variable</strong> in a VAE-like framework</li>
<li>Jointly learns an approximate posterior over <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0077em;">R</span></span></span></span> and an imitator policy</li>
<li>Entirely offline, no inner-loop MDP solver needed</li>
<li>Limitation: variational approximation may underestimate posterior uncertainty</li>
</ul>

<h3 id="active-learning-with-birl">Active Learning with BIRL<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#active-learning-with-birl">#</a></h3>
<h4 id="information-theoretic-query-selection">Information-Theoretic Query Selection<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#information-theoretic-query-selection">#</a></h4>
<p>BIRL provides a natural framework for <strong>active IRL</strong>: querying the expert at maximally informative states rather than passively collecting demonstrations.</p>
<p>The standard acquisition function is the <strong>Expected Information Gain (EIG)</strong>:</p>
<span class="katex-display"><span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord <h3 id="advanced-extensions">Advanced Extensions<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#advanced-extensions">#</a></h3>
<h4 id="non-markovian-rewards">Non-Markovian Rewards<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#non-markovian-rewards">#</a></h4>
<p>Standard IRL assumes the reward is Markovian: <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0077em;">R</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal">s</span><span class="mpunct">,</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">a</span><span class="mclose">)</span></span></span></span> depends only on the current state-action pair. Recent work extends BIRL to <strong>Reward Machines (RMs)</strong> — finite-state machines encoding history-dependent (non-Markovian) reward functions.</p>
<p>The key changes:</p>
<ul>
<li>Hypothesis space becomes the space of RMs (finite automata over <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.7667em;vertical-align:-0.0833em;"></span><span class="mord mathcal" style="margin-right:0.075em;">S</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2222em;"></span><span class="mbin">×</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2222em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathcal">A</span></span></span></span>)</li>
<li>Demonstration is projected onto the state space of each product MDP <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.7667em;vertical-align:-0.0833em;"></span><span class="mord mathcal">M</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2222em;"></span><span class="mbin">×</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2222em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathcal">R</span></span></span></span></li>
<li>MAP estimation via <strong>simulated annealing</strong> (continuous gradient methods unavailable for discrete RM structure)</li>
</ul>
<h4 id="bayesian-theory-of-mind-btom-and-robust-birl">Bayesian Theory of Mind (BTOM) and Robust BIRL<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#bayesian-theory-of-mind-btom-and-robust-birl">#</a></h4>
<p>Standard BIRL assumes the expert's internal model of dynamics equals the true model <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.1389em;">T</span></span></span></span>. <strong>BTOM</strong> (Baker et al. 2011; Wei et al. 2023) simultaneously infers:</p>
<ul>
<li>The expert's reward <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0077em;">R</span></span></span></span></li>

<h2 id="cooperative-inverse-reinforcement-learning-cirl">Cooperative Inverse Reinforcement Learning (CIRL)<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#cooperative-inverse-reinforcement-learning-cirl">#</a></h2>
<h3 id="motivation-and-the-value-alignment-problem">Motivation and the Value Alignment Problem<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#motivation-and-the-value-alignment-problem">#</a></h3>
<p>Classical IRL and BIRL share a fundamental architectural assumption: the human and robot are <strong>decoupled</strong>. The human acts as if the robot doesn't exist (the <em>Demonstration By Expert</em> assumption), and the robot passively observes. CIRL (Hadfield-Menell, Dragan, Abbeel &#x26; Russell, NeurIPS 2016) challenges this assumption by formalizing the <strong>value alignment problem</strong> as a cooperative two-player game.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"We had better be quite sure that the purpose put into the machine is the purpose which we really desire." — Norbert Wiener, 1960</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The King Midas problem operationalized: specifying an incorrect reward function leads to catastrophically wrong behavior even from a well-intentioned specification. CIRL proposes that the robot should not be given a fixed reward function at all — it should <strong>learn</strong> the human's reward function through cooperative interaction.</p>
<h4 id="the-core-departure-from-classical-irl">The Core Departure from Classical IRL<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#the-core-departure-from-classical-irl">#</a></h4>
<blockquote>
<p>In classical IRL the robot is a <strong>passive observer</strong> and the human acts <strong>optimally in isolation</strong>. In CIRL, both agents are <strong>active participants</strong> and the human's optimal strategy may involve <em>deliberately suboptimal actions</em> to better communicate the reward to the robot.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>This is a profound shift: the human's role changes from expert demonstrator to <strong>teacher</strong>, and the robot's role changes from reward inferrer to <strong>cooperative learner</strong>. The key contribution is proving that the classical IRL assumption (Demonstration By Expert, DBE) is not just an approximation — it is formally <strong>suboptimal</strong> within the CIRL game.</p>

</blockquote>
<p>Intuition: under DBE the human greedily collects reward without regard for informativeness. An optimal human teacher may accept a lower immediate reward in order to signal which states have high reward — making the robot's inference task much easier. The robot learns more, and the joint long-run reward is higher.</p>
<h3 id="reduction-to-pomdp">Reduction to POMDP<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#reduction-to-pomdp">#</a></h3>
<h4 id="cirl-game-pomdp">CIRL Game → POMDP<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#cirl-game-pomdp">#</a></h4>
<p>Computing optimal joint policies in a cooperative game is, in general, the problem of solving a <strong>Dec-POMDP</strong> (Decentralized Partially Observable MDP) — which is <strong>NEXP-complete</strong>. CIRL's special structure rescues this.</p>
<p><strong>Key structural insight</strong>: <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0813em;">H</span></span></span></span> has <strong>full state information</strong> (she knows <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">θ</span></span></span></span> and observes <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">s</span></span></span></span>); only <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0077em;">R</span></span></span></span> has partial information (it doesn't know <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">θ</span></span></span></span>). This asymmetry means the CIRL game can be reduced to a <strong>single-agent POMDP</strong> from <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0077em;">R</span></span></span></span>'s perspective:</p>

<h3 id="the-dbe-suboptimality-a-closer-look">The DBE Suboptimality: A Closer Look<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#the-dbe-suboptimality-a-closer-look">#</a></h3>
<h4 id="instructive-vs-optimal-demonstrations">Instructive vs. Optimal Demonstrations<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#instructive-vs-optimal-demonstrations">#</a></h4>
<p>The qualitative behavior that emerges from optimal CIRL solutions is distinct from classical IRL input:</p>



































<table><thead><tr><th align="left">Behavior Type</th><th align="left">Classical IRL (DBE)</th><th align="left">Optimal CIRL</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td align="left"><strong>Human strategy</strong></td><td align="left">Greedy-optimal in isolation</td><td align="left">Pedagogically optimal (may sacrifice reward)</td></tr><tr><td align="left"><strong>Robot strategy</strong></td><td align="left">Passive inference from demonstrations</td><td align="left">Active learning, queries, and information-seeking</td></tr><tr><td align="left"><strong>Communication</strong></td><td align="left">One-directional (H → R)</td><td align="left">Bidirectional implicit communication</td></tr><tr><td align="left"><strong>Active teaching</strong></td><td align="left">Never emerges</td><td align="left">Emerges naturally as optimal behavior</td></tr><tr><td align="left"><strong>Legible motion</strong></td><td align="left">Not incentivized</td><td align="left">Directly incentivized</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Concretely: in a gridworld experiment, under DBE the human walks directly to the highest-reward cell (efficient, but uninformative about the reward landscape). Under optimal CIRL the human <em>detours</em> through other high-reward regions, sacrificing a few steps to give <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0077em;">R</span></span></span></span> a richer signal — and the joint policy achieves substantially higher long-run value.</p>
<h4 id="the-two-phase-apprenticeship-formulation">The Two-Phase Apprenticeship Formulation<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#the-two-phase-apprenticeship-formulation">#</a></h4>
<p>CIRL formalizes apprenticeship learning as a <strong>two-phase turn-based game</strong>:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Learning phase</strong>: Both <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0813em;">H</span></span></span></span> and <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0077em;">R</span></span></span></span> take actions; <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0077em;">R</span></span></span></span> updates its belief <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">b</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">θ</span><span class="mclose">)</span></span></span></span></li>
<li><strong>Deployment phase</strong>: <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0077em;">R</span></span></span></span> acts alone using its learned belief to maximize <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.9468em;"></span><span class="mord accent"><span class="vlist-t"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.9468em;"><span style="top:-3em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:3em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0077em;">R</span></span><span style="top:-3.2523em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:3em;"></span><span class="accent-body" style="left:-0.1667em;"><span class="mord">^</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></li>
</ul>
<p>Classical IRL is exactly the robot's best response in the deployment phase when <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0813em;">H</span></span></span></span> played DBE in the learning phase. The CIRL result says: even if <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0077em;">R</span></span></span></span> expects <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0813em;">H</span></span></span></span> to play DBE, <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0813em;">H</span></span></span></span> should <em>not</em> play DBE — she can do better by playing an instructive strategy instead.</p>
<h3 id="cirl-and-safety-the-off-switch-game">CIRL and Safety: The Off-Switch Game<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#cirl-and-safety-the-off-switch-game">#</a></h3>
<h4 id="corrigibility-from-reward-uncertainty">Corrigibility from Reward Uncertainty<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#corrigibility-from-reward-uncertainty">#</a></h4>
<p>One of the most important implications of CIRL for AI safety (Hadfield-Menell et al. 2017, "The Off-Switch Game") is the following insight:</p>
<blockquote>
<p><strong>Theorem</strong>: An agent optimizing a CIRL objective — uncertain about the human's reward function — will <em>voluntarily accept being shut down</em>, because shutdown may signal that its current behavior is misaligned with <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">θ</span></span></span></span>.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Formally, if <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0077em;">R</span></span></span></span> maintains uncertainty <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">b</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">θ</span><span class="mclose">)</span></span></span></span> and <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0813em;">H</span></span></span></span> presses a shutdown button, this is evidence that <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0077em;">R</span></span></span></span>'s current policy is not maximizing <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1.1968em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord accent"><span class="vlist-t"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.9468em;"><span style="top:-3em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:3em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0077em;">R</span></span><span style="top:-3.2523em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:3em;"></span><span class="accent-body" style="left:-0.1667em;"><span class="mord">^</span></span></span></span></span></span></span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord">⋅</span><span class="mpunct">;</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">θ</span><span class="mclose">)</span></span></span></span>. A rational <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0077em;">R</span></span></span></span> updates its belief and defers. In contrast, an agent with a <strong>fixed</strong> reward function has a strong instrumental incentive to <em>prevent</em> shutdown (it cannot achieve its objective if turned off). This is the classic <strong>self-preservation problem</strong> of classical RL agents.</p>
<p><strong>The mechanism</strong>: reward uncertainty creates an incentive to learn more about <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">θ</span></span></span></span> — including from shutdown signals. Uncertainty is the source of corrigibility, not an external constraint.</p>
<h4 id="limits-of-cirl-corrigibility">Limits of CIRL Corrigibility<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#limits-of-cirl-corrigibility">#</a></h4>
<p>This result is not robust to all assumptions. Critical failure modes identified in follow-up work:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Model misspecification</strong>: If the prior <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.1389em;">P</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t vlist-t2"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.3011em;"><span style="top:-2.55em;margin-left:-0.1389em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mord mtight">0</span></span></span></span><span class="vlist-s">​</span></span><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.15em;"><span></span></span></span></span></span></span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">θ</span><span class="mclose">)</span></span></span></span> or the parametric family <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1.1968em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord accent"><span class="vlist-t"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.9468em;"><span style="top:-3em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:3em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0077em;">R</span></span><span style="top:-3.2523em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:3em;"></span><span class="accent-body" style="left:-0.1667em;"><span class="mord">^</span></span></span></span></span></span></span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord">⋅</span><span class="mpunct">;</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">θ</span><span class="mclose">)</span></span></span></span> is wrong, <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0077em;">R</span></span></span></span> may have beliefs that cause it to <em>interpret</em> shutdown commands as noise rather than information — and resist them.</li>
<li><strong>Fully updated deference</strong>: As <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord">∣</span><span class="mord mathcal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">D</span><span class="mord">∣</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">→</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord">∞</span></span></span></span>, <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0077em;">R</span></span></span></span> may become fully confident in its inferred <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">θ</span></span></span></span>. At that point uncertainty vanishes and so does the corrigibility incentive — this is the <strong>fully updated deference</strong> problem.</li>
<li><strong>Single-shot games</strong>: Corrigibility properties in CIRL rely on repeated interaction. In one-shot settings the incentive to defer weakens substantially.</li>
</ul>
<blockquote>
<p>CIRL corrigibility is <strong>emergent from uncertainty</strong>, not a hardcoded guarantee. It is fragile to model misspecification and disappears as the agent becomes more confident. It should be treated as a useful framework, not a solved problem.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The alignment research community largely views CIRL as a valuable problem formalization rather than a complete solution — the gap between the idealized CIRL model and real-world AI systems remains large.</p>
<h3 id="birl-vs-cirl-how-they-relate">BIRL vs. CIRL: How They Relate<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#birl-vs-cirl-how-they-relate">#</a></h3>
<h4 id="conceptual-comparison">Conceptual Comparison<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#conceptual-comparison">#</a></h4>
<p>CIRL strictly generalizes and reframes the BIRL setting:</p>













































<table><thead><tr><th align="left">Dimension</th><th align="left">BIRL</th><th align="left">CIRL</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td align="left"><strong>Agent structure</strong></td><td align="left">Robot only; human is passive data source</td><td align="left">Both human and robot are active agents</td></tr><tr><td align="left"><strong>Human model</strong></td><td align="left">Fixed Boltzmann-rational demonstrator</td><td align="left">Strategic co-player aware of robot's learning</td></tr><tr><td align="left"><strong>Robot's role</strong></td><td align="left">Posterior inference over <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0077em;">R</span></span></span></span></td><td align="left">Cooperative policy optimization under partial info</td></tr><tr><td align="left"><strong>Human's role</strong></td><td align="left">Demonstrate (DBE assumed)</td><td align="left">Teach (pedagogically optimal strategy)</td></tr><tr><td align="left"><strong>Formal framework</strong></td><td align="left">Bayesian inference / MCMC</td><td align="left">Dec-POMDP reduced to single-agent POMDP</td></tr><tr><td align="left"><strong>Safety implications</strong></td><td align="left">Uncertainty for robust policy</td><td align="left">Uncertainty as source of corrigibility</td></tr><tr><td align="left"><strong>Optimality</strong></td><td align="left">Robot is optimal given DBE assumption</td><td align="left">Joint optimality without DBE assumption</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Both frameworks maintain a <strong>belief over the reward function</strong> — this is the shared core. BIRL is the robot's problem in isolation; CIRL asks what happens when you take the human's strategy seriously as a co-optimization.</p>
<h4 id="cirl-as-a-generalization-of-birl">CIRL as a Generalization of BIRL<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#cirl-as-a-generalization-of-birl">#</a></h4>
<p>Classical BIRL falls out of CIRL as follows. Fix <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.5806em;vertical-align:-0.15em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0359em;">π</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t vlist-t2"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.3283em;"><span style="top:-2.55em;margin-left:-0.0359em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mord mathnormal mtight" style="margin-right:0.0813em;">H</span></span></span></span><span class="vlist-s">​</span></span><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.15em;"><span></span></span></span></span></span></span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">=</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1.1167em;vertical-align:-0.2753em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0359em;">π</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t vlist-t2"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.8413em;"><span style="top:-2.4247em;margin-left:-0.0359em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mord mathnormal mtight" style="margin-right:0.0813em;">H</span></span></span><span style="top:-3.063em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mord mtight"><span class="mord text mtight"><span class="mord mtight">DBE</span></span></span></span></span></span><span class="vlist-s">​</span></span><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.2753em;"><span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span> (human plays optimally in isolation, Boltzmann-rational). Then the robot's problem reduces exactly to:</p>
<span class="katex-display"><span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">p</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">θ</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">∣</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord mathcal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">D</span><span class="mclose">)</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">∝</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">p</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathcal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">D</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">∣</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">θ</span><span class="mclose">)</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">p</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">θ</span><span class="mclose">)</span></span></span></span></span>
<p>which is the BIRL posterior with reward parameter <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">θ</span></span></span></span>. So BIRL is the <strong>best response to a fixed, non-strategic human</strong>. CIRL asks for the joint optimum where both sides adapt to each other. In practice, BIRL is more tractable; CIRL is more correct.</p>

<h4 id="relaxing-human-rationality">Relaxing Human Rationality<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#relaxing-human-rationality">#</a></h4>
<p>A key limitation of vanilla CIRL is that <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0813em;">H</span></span></span></span> must be optimal — a strong assumption. The modified Bellman update approach also allows relaxing this: instead of requiring <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0813em;">H</span></span></span></span> to be fully optimal, it is sufficient that <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0813em;">H</span></span></span></span>'s policy is <strong>parametrized by her Q-values</strong> (a weaker condition), enabling more realistic human models including bounded rationality and cognitive biases. See also Bayesian Theory of Mind for a related approach.</p>
<h3 id="extensions-and-open-problems">Extensions and Open Problems<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#extensions-and-open-problems">#</a></h3>
<h4 id="multi-human-cirl-and-social-choice">Multi-Human CIRL and Social Choice<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#multi-human-cirl-and-social-choice">#</a></h4>
<p>The basic CIRL game has a single human with reward <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">θ</span></span></span></span>. Extensions to multiple humans <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mopen">{</span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0813em;">H</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t vlist-t2"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.3117em;"><span style="top:-2.55em;margin-left:-0.0813em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mord mathnormal mtight">i</span></span></span></span><span class="vlist-s">​</span></span><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.15em;"><span></span></span></span></span></span></span><span class="mclose">}</span></span></span></span> with competing rewards <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mopen">{</span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">θ</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t vlist-t2"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.3117em;"><span style="top:-2.55em;margin-left:-0.0278em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mord mathnormal mtight">i</span></span></span></span><span class="vlist-s">​</span></span><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.15em;"><span></span></span></span></span></span></span><span class="mclose">}</span></span></span></span> raise social choice problems: whose preferences should <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0077em;">R</span></span></span></span> optimize? One direction (Fickinger et al.) uses shared control as a social welfare aggregation mechanism. This connects CIRL to Mechanism Design and Multi-Agent RL, relevant for settings like AI assistants interacting with groups rather than individuals — directly relevant for multi-agent AI safety research.</p>
<h4 id="cirl-with-partial-human-self-knowledge">CIRL with Partial Human Self-Knowledge<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#cirl-with-partial-human-self-knowledge">#</a></h4>
<p>Humans often do not know their own preferences perfectly — they may be uncertain about <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">θ</span></span></span></span> themselves. Extensions of CIRL where <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0813em;">H</span></span></span></span> also has a belief <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathnormal">b</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t vlist-t2"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.3283em;"><span style="top:-2.55em;margin-left:0em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mord mathnormal mtight" style="margin-right:0.0813em;">H</span></span></span></span><span class="vlist-s">​</span></span><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.15em;"><span></span></span></span></span></span></span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">θ</span><span class="mclose">)</span></span></span></span> (with <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0813em;">H</span></span></span></span> observing <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">θ</span></span></span></span> noisily) lead to a <strong>both-sided partial information game</strong>, a fully symmetric Dec-POMDP. This is computationally much harder but more realistic for human-AI alignment scenarios.</p>
<h4 id="connection-to-rlhf">Connection to RLHF<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#connection-to-rlhf">#</a></h4>
<p>Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) can be viewed as an approximate, scalable instantiation of the CIRL idea:</p>
<ul>
<li>Human preference labels are the "demonstrations" providing evidence about <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">θ</span></span></span></span></li>
<li>A reward model is trained to approximate the posterior mean of <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">p</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">θ</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">∣</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord text"><span class="mord">preferences</span></span><span class="mclose">)</span></span></span></span></li>
<li>The policy is then optimized against the reward model</li>
</ul>
<p>RLHF discards the cooperative game structure and epistemic uncertainty of CIRL for scalability. CIRL provides the theoretical grounding that RLHF practitioners implicitly rely on; RLHF provides the scalability that CIRL lacks. The gap between them is an active research frontier.</p>
