30:T36393,<h2 id="the-spectral-theorem-a-theorem-chain-construction">The Spectral Theorem: A Theorem-Chain Construction<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#the-spectral-theorem-a-theorem-chain-construction">#</a></h2>
<h3 id="strategic-overview">Strategic Overview<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#strategic-overview">#</a></h3>
<p>The spectral theorem is not one theorem but a <strong>family</strong>. Here we build the finite-dimensional versions — both for <strong>self-adjoint operators on real inner product spaces</strong> and <strong>normal operators on complex inner product spaces</strong> — and end by sketching the path to the infinite-dimensional (bounded / unbounded / compact) generalizations.</p>
<p>Two genuinely different proof routes are possible. The thing to remember is that the <strong>complex case is cleaner</strong> because the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra hands you an eigenvalue for free; the <strong>real case requires an extra trick</strong> (complexification or a quadratic-factor argument) to extract an eigenvalue from a self-adjoint operator.</p>

<h4 id="complex-case-eigenvalues-always-exist">Complex Case: Eigenvalues Always Exist<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#complex-case-eigenvalues-always-exist">#</a></h4>
<p>Over <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6889em;"></span><span class="mord mathbb">C</span></span></span></span>, the characteristic polynomial <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mop">det</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.1389em;">T</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2222em;"></span><span class="mbin">−</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2222em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">λ</span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0785em;">I</span><span class="mclose">)</span></span></span></span> has a root by Fundamental Theorem of Algebra. <h4 id="unbounded-self-adjoint-operators">Unbounded Self-Adjoint Operators<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#unbounded-self-adjoint-operators">#</a></h4>
<p>Now domains matter. The right notion is <strong>essentially self-adjoint</strong> (closure equals adjoint). The spectral theorem still holds — this is what justifies quantum-mechanical observables like position and momentum even though they're unbounded.</p>
<h3 id="connections-synthesis">Connections &#x26; Synthesis<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#connections-synthesis">#</a></h3>
<h4 id="why-normal-is-the-right-class">Why Normal Is The Right Class<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#why-normal-is-the-right-class">#</a></h4>
<p>The pattern is: <strong>diagonalizability in an ONB</strong> = <strong>commutation of the operator with its adjoint</strong>. The structural reason is that an eigen-ONB simultaneously diagonalizes <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.1389em;">T</span></span></span></span> and <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6887em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.1389em;">T</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.6887em;"><span style="top:-3.063em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mbin mtight">∗</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span>, and any two matrices diagonal in the same basis commute. The converse — that commutation forces simultaneous diagonalization — is exactly the spectral theorem.</p>

<li><strong>Graph Laplacian methods</strong> (spectral clustering, Cheeger inequality): <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">L</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">=</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.7667em;vertical-align:-0.0833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">D</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2222em;"></span><span class="mbin">−</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2222em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">A</span></span></span></span> is self-adjoint, eigenvectors of small eigenvalues encode community structure.</li>
