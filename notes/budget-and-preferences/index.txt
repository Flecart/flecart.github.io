<h3 id="a-definition-for-budget">A definition for Budget<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#a-definition-for-budget">#</a></h3>
<p>Economist want simple models to start to model things. One of the things we will model here is how do you describe what you can afford about some goods.</p>

<h4 id="opportunity-cost">Opportunity Cost<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#opportunity-cost">#</a></h4>
<p>Introducing this budget set enables us to introduce one important concept in economy.
Suppose we just have two goods. Then, if you buy more goods of one, you need to buy less of the other. This is called <strong>opportunity cost</strong>. The opportunity cost of buying one more unit of good <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6444em;"></span><span class="mord">1</span></span></span></span> is the amount of good <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6444em;"></span><span class="mord">2</span></span></span></span> you need to give up to buy that unit. In this case, this is given by the slope of the budget constraint.</p>
<p>The opportunity cost is a more general concept that can be applied everywhere in life. Time is a clear budgeted item: if you choose an activity, you are giving up the opportunity to do another, so you say, there is a <em>cost</em> in choosing that specific activity.</p>

<h2 id="preferences">Preferences<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#preferences">#</a></h2>
<p>In the previous section, we have described what a consumer can <em>afford</em>. In this section, we describe what he/she <em>prefers</em>.
Originally, the preferences where described by <strong>utility functions</strong>. These functions can be seen as one way to encode preferences. The only important thing about utility was the ease of comparison, not their magnitude, which had not a clear interpretation. Another problem with these kinds of functions was the difficulty of assigning a number to them. Note that if it was only their relative ordering that matters, then one can apply every monotonic function to them, and it would still hold!</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Given suﬃcient observations on consumer choices it will often be possible to estimate the utility function that generated those choices.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>One can see that finding the perfect choice is quite similar to <a href="/notes/optimization-methods/">Optimization methods</a>.</p>
<h3 id="consumption-bundles">Consumption bundles<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#consumption-bundles">#</a></h3>
<h4 id="what-is-a-consumption-bundle">What is a Consumption Bundle?<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#what-is-a-consumption-bundle">#</a></h4>
<p>A bundle is just a set of Goods.</p>
<h4 id="axioms-of-preferences">Axioms of Preferences<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#axioms-of-preferences">#</a></h4>
<p>In this subsection, we will define the vocabulary for preferences.
Given two consumption bundles <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">x</span></span></span></span> and <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.625em;vertical-align:-0.1944em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0359em;">y</span></span></span></span> we define them to be:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Strictly preferred</strong>: if <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.5782em;vertical-align:-0.0391em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">x</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">≺</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.625em;vertical-align:-0.1944em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0359em;">y</span></span></span></span></li>

I have no idea why we assume consumption bundles are considered to be continuous. Intuitively, I would say that two different goods are hardly comparable with each other.
Probably it makes sense to thing about indifference curves when we are talking about a <em>quantity</em> of some specific goods. This is the case that is intended here. The drawback is that we cannot compare different set of goods, but only different assignments of the same set of goods.</p>
<h4 id="indifference-curves-do-not-cross">Indifference Curves do not Cross<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#indifference-curves-do-not-cross">#</a></h4>
<p>If two indifference curves cross, then you can find a bundle that is both strictly preferred to another and indifferent, which is a contradiction to the axioms of preferences.</p>

#### Marginal Rate of Substitution
The Marginal Rate of Substitution is the rate at which a consumer is willing to trade one good for another. It is defined as the **slope** of the indifference curve at a certain point:</span>
<p>MRS_{x_{1} \rightarrow x_{2}} = -\frac{d x_{1}}{d x_{2}}</p>
<span class="katex-error" title="ParseError: KaTeX parse error: Can&#x27;t use function &#x27;$&#x27; in math mode at position 71: …ubstitute good $̲x_{1}$ for good…" style="color:#cc0000">Which is the rate at which the customer is willing to substitute good $x_{1}$ for good $x_{2}$ at a point $x_{1}$ of the indifference curve.

This value is sometimes useful to model the trade propensity for a certain customer. If the rate of exchange is exactly the MRS, then the customer is sayd to be *on the margin* of being willing to substitue the goods, this can be also interpreted as a **transaction** for good 2 by paying with good 1.

> Convexity of indiﬀerence curves seems very natural: it says that the more you have of one good, the more willing you are to give some of it up in exchange for the other good.

If the goods have prices, then the ratio between these prices should be the MRS.

#### Marginal Utility
The marginal utility with respect to some good is the amount of change in the utility of one function when changing the quantity of that good by one unit.</span>
<p>MU_{x_{1}} = \frac{d u(x)}{d x_{1}}</p>
