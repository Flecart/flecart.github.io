<h3 id="knee-jerk-response">Knee-Jerk Response<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#knee-jerk-response">#</a></h3>
<p><img src="/images/notes/Neural mechanisms-20250217161536263.webp" width="649" class="center" alt="Neural mechanisms-20250217161536263"></p>
<p>The <strong>knee-jerk reflex</strong> (also known as the <strong>patellar reflex</strong>) is a classic example of a <strong>mono-synaptic reflex arc</strong>, which involves a direct connection between <em>sensory and motor neurons</em>, as well as inhibitory circuits to regulate movement.
This is important for us if we fall and hit something.</p>
<h4 id="neural-circuit-of-the-knee-jerk-reflex">Neural Circuit of the Knee-Jerk Reflex<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#neural-circuit-of-the-knee-jerk-reflex">#</a></h4>
<p>The reflex involves the following components:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Sensory Neuron (Afferent Pathway)</strong>
<ul>
<li>When the patellar tendon is tapped, <strong>muscle spindles</strong> in the quadriceps detect the sudden stretch.</li>
<li>This activates <strong>Ia afferent fibers</strong>, which send an electrical signal to the spinal cord.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Interneuron (Inhibitory Pathway)</strong>
<ul>
<li>Within the spinal cord, the afferent neuron synapses onto an <strong>inhibitory interneuron</strong>.</li>
<li>This interneuron, in turn, inhibits the <strong>motor neuron controlling the antagonist muscle (flexor muscle, hamstring)</strong> to prevent opposing contraction.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Motor Neuron (Efferent Pathway to Extensor Muscle)</strong>
<ul>
<li>The <strong>direct monosynaptic connection</strong> between the sensory neuron and the <strong>alpha motor neuron</strong> in the spinal cord leads to activation of the quadriceps muscle.</li>
<li>This results in contraction of the <strong>extensor muscle</strong> (quadriceps), producing the characteristic leg kick.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Motor Neuron (Efferent Pathway to Flexor Muscle - Inhibition)</strong>
<ul>
<li>The inhibitory interneuron prevents contraction of the <strong>antagonist muscle</strong> (hamstring), ensuring smooth movement.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>
<p>This reflex demonstrates the fundamental organization of <strong>afferent (sensory) and efferent (motor) pathways</strong>, where information flows toward the central nervous system (CNS) via afferents and away from it via efferents.</p>
<p>The inhibitory interneuron releases <strong>neurotransmitters like GABA or glycine</strong>, which cause <strong>hyperpolarization</strong> of the flexor motor neuron.</p>
<ul>
<li>This <strong>inhibits the hamstring contraction</strong>, allowing smooth knee extension.</li>
<li>The hyperpolarization prevents unwanted co-contraction of opposing muscles.</li>
</ul>
<h4 id="recording-methods-for-neural-activity">Recording Methods for Neural Activity<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#recording-methods-for-neural-activity">#</a></h4>
<p>Neural activity can be recorded during reflex responses, including in <strong>clinical and surgical settings</strong>.<br>
There are two primary methods:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Extracellular Recording with Microelectrodes</strong>
<ul>
<li>A <strong>glass micro-electrode with a super-thin metal tip</strong> is used to measure the voltage difference between the inside and outside of a neuron.</li>
<li>This is mainly used to record <strong>action potentials</strong> or <strong>field potentials</strong> from neurons.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Intracellular Current Injection</strong>
<ul>
<li>A current can be injected into the neuron to measure its <strong>passive properties</strong> (such as resistance and capacitance) and <strong>active properties</strong> (such as action potential threshold and firing patterns).</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>
<blockquote>
<p>Intracellular recordings can detect the smaller, graded potential
changes that trigger action potentials, and thus allow a more detailed analy-
sis of communication between neurons within a circuit.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>In a similar way, we measure the <em>myotatic</em> circuits in the heart.</p>
<p>These techniques have also been applied in the <strong>human brain during surgery</strong>, such as in procedures for <strong>epilepsy treatment</strong> or <strong>deep brain stimulation</strong>, where patients can report their sensations in real time while neuronal activity is recorded.</p>
<h4 id="activations-in-the-knee-jerk-response">Activations in the Knee-Jerk response<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#activations-in-the-knee-jerk-response">#</a></h4>
<p><img src="/images/notes/Neural mechanisms-20250820203502916.webp" style="width: 100%" class="center" alt="Neural mechanisms-20250820203502916"></p>
<h4 id="receptive-fields">Receptive Fields<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#receptive-fields">#</a></h4>
<p>The <strong>receptive field</strong> of a neuron refers to the specific region of the body (e.g., skin, retina) that, when stimulated, makes the neuron fire.</p>
<p>For example:</p>
<ul>
<li>In the <strong>somatosensory system</strong>, a mechanoreceptor in the skin will fire when a specific area is touched.</li>
<li>In the <strong>visual system</strong>, a retinal ganglion cell has a receptive field defined by a portion of the visual space.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Key properties of receptive fields:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Size and resolution</strong>: Smaller receptive fields (e.g., in fingertips) allow <strong>higher spatial resolution</strong> and finer touch discrimination.</li>
<li><strong>Excitatory and inhibitory zones</strong>: Many neurons have center-surround organization, where stimulation of the center <strong>excites</strong> the neuron, while stimulation of the surrounding area <strong>inhibits</strong> it.</li>
</ul>
<p>The single neuron electrophysiological recording is opposed to functional brain imaging.</p>
<h2 id="electrical-signals-in-neurons"><strong>Electrical Signals in Neurons</strong><a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#electrical-signals-in-neurons">#</a></h2>
<p>Neurons communicate through <strong>electrical signals</strong> that arise due to the movement of <strong>ions</strong> across the cell membrane. This is achieved through:</p>
<h3 id="types-of-potentials-and-signals">Types of potentials and signals<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#types-of-potentials-and-signals">#</a></h3>
<p>In general, action potentials are divided in two phases, a first Na phase and second potassium phase.</p>
<h4 id="potentials">Potentials<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#potentials">#</a></h4>
<ol>
<li><strong>Receptor Potentials</strong> (input signals from sensory receptors)</li>
<li><strong>Synaptic Potentials</strong> (signals from other neurons)</li>
<li><strong>Action Potentials</strong> (output signals transmitted down the axon)</li>
</ol>
<blockquote>
<p>A fundamental problem for neurons is that their axons, which can be quite long (remember that a spinal motor neuron can extend for a meter or more), are not good electrical conductors. [...] To compensate for this deficiency, neurons have evolved a “booster system” that allows them to conduct electrical signals over great distances despite their intrinsically poor electrical characteristics. he electrical signals produced by this booster system are called action potentials</p>
</blockquote>

<h4 id="role-of-sodium">Role of sodium<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#role-of-sodium">#</a></h4>
<p><img src="/images/notes/Neural mechanisms-20250819231347203.webp" style="width: 100%" class="center" alt="Neural mechanisms-20250819231347203">
Sodium is important to have an early inward membrane current.
Then we have a potassium current outside and tetraetilammonium blocks that.</p>
<p>The conductance of those two follow a sigmoid with respect to membrane potential.
The speed by which they activate is different.
Potassium channels are a lot slower (about 1ms delay with respect with the Na channels).</p>

<p><img src="/images/notes/Neural mechanisms-20250819231452361.webp" style="width: 100%" class="center" alt="Neural mechanisms-20250819231452361"></p>
<h4 id="depolarization-and-hyperpolarization">Depolarization and Hyperpolarization<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#depolarization-and-hyperpolarization">#</a></h4>
<ul>
<li>At rest, neurons are in a <strong>hyperpolarized state</strong> (negative membrane potential, typically around -70 mV).
<ul>
<li>When a negative current is passed, the cell membrane just addapts <strong>passively</strong> and becomes a little bit less negative.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>When they receive input (e.g., touch, neurotransmitters), they become <strong>depolarized</strong> (more positive).</li>
<li>If the depolarization reaches a threshold, called <strong>threshold potential</strong>, an <strong>action potential</strong> is triggered, and we see a spike. The number of spikes is related to the intensity of the action potential.</li>
</ul>

<h3 id="the-hodgkin-huxley-model">The Hodgkin-Huxley Model<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#the-hodgkin-huxley-model">#</a></h3>
<p>Alan Hodgkin and Andrew Huxley pioneered the study of <strong>action potentials</strong> in neurons using <strong>electrophysiology</strong>.</p>
<h4 id="experimental-setting">Experimental Setting<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#experimental-setting">#</a></h4>
<ul>
<li>They inserted <strong>electrodes into the axons of the giant squid</strong> (which has thick axons ~800 μm in diameter, ideal for measurements, hundreths of times larger compared to mammalian axons).
<ul>
<li>They are larger for better conductivity, which helped them having faster reaction times.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Measured <strong>how ion permeability changes over time</strong> during an action potential.</li>
<li>Demonstrated that <strong>K⁺ is the dominant ion at rest</strong>, while <strong>Na⁺ permeability increases during an action potential</strong>, and returns with dominant K at rest (higher concentration in the cell than outside).</li>
</ul>
<h4 id="key-findings">Key Findings<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#key-findings">#</a></h4>
<ol>
<li><strong>Ion Concentration Dependence</strong>
<ul>
<li>They determined that the action potential <strong>depends on Na⁺ and K⁺ gradients</strong>, but <strong>not Cl⁻</strong>.</li>
<li>Increasing external <strong>K⁺ concentration disrupts the resting potential</strong>, making neurons more likely to fire spontaneously.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Voltage-Dependent Ion Channels</strong>
<ul>
<li><strong>Na⁺ channels open at depolarization</strong> (threshold level ~ -55 mV).</li>
<li><strong>K⁺ channels open later</strong> and restore resting potential.</li>
<li>Both channels <strong>show voltage-dependent gating</strong>:
<ul>
<li>Na⁺ channels open <strong>rapidly</strong>, allowing <strong>inward Na⁺ current</strong> (depolarization).</li>
<li>K⁺ channels open <strong>slower</strong>, allowing <strong>outward K⁺ current</strong> (repolarization).</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Logarithmic Graph of Potassium vs. Energy Level</strong>
<ul>
<li>Hodgkin and Huxley plotted <strong>log K⁺ vs. voltage</strong>, showing a predictable relationship (consistent with the <strong>Nernst equation</strong>).</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Mathematical Modeling of Action Potentials</strong>
<ul>
<li>They wrote a <strong>set of differential equations</strong> describing ion channel kinetics.</li>
<li>These equations <strong>successfully predicted experimental results</strong>, marking the beginning of <strong>computational neuroscience</strong>.</li>
<li>The equations describe:
<ul>
<li><strong>Membrane capacitance</strong> (how the membrane stores charge).</li>
<li><strong>Ion conductance</strong> (how easily ions pass through).</li>
<li><strong>Time-dependent changes</strong> in channel permeability.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Sodium Permeability and Action Potentials</strong>
<ul>
<li>Action potentials depend on a <strong>higher concentration of Na⁺ outside the cell</strong>.</li>
<li>Hodgkin and Huxley confirmed that <strong>removing extracellular Na⁺ eliminates action potentials</strong>.</li>
<li><strong>Cl⁻ was shown to be unimportant</strong> for action potential generation.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>
<h4 id="resting-membrane-potential">Resting Membrane Potential<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#resting-membrane-potential">#</a></h4>
<p>Resting membrane potential mostly depends on the concentration of the ion in the extracellular space.
The resting membrane potential depends mainly on the extracellular potassium concentration.</p>
<p>We break the electric gradient if there is too much potassium on the outside. On resting conditions potassium is the main variable.</p>

<h4 id="role-of-sodium-in-the-action-potential">Role of Sodium in the Action potential<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#role-of-sodium-in-the-action-potential">#</a></h4>
<p>The shape of the membrane current depends upon sodium.
With more sodium, the process is faster, the spike is a lot larger (they experimented with more sodium in extracellular environment).
The above are the main conclusions.

<h2 id="action-potentials">Action potentials<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#action-potentials">#</a></h2>
<h3 id="action-potential-a-rapid-electrical-signal">Action Potential: A Rapid Electrical Signal<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#action-potential-a-rapid-electrical-signal">#</a></h3>
<p>An <strong>action potential</strong> is a rapid, <em>transient</em> change in membrane voltage that propagates along the <strong>axon</strong>. It is fundamental to neural communication and muscle contraction.</p>
<h4 id="different-action-potential-shapes">Different action potential shapes<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#different-action-potential-shapes">#</a></h4>
<p>Why the action potential mechanism?</p>
<ul>
<li>Timing (precision)</li>
<li>Generative aspect (avalanche of signals, not passive propagation but repropagation)</li>
<li>It is fast?</li>
</ul>
<p></p><figure class="center">
<img src="/images/notes/Neural mechanisms-20250822091836211.webp" style="width: 100%" alt="Neural mechanisms-20250822091836211" title="Neural mechanisms-20250822091836211">
<figcaption><p style="text-align:center;">Action potential shapes can vary across neurons: A) squid giant axon, B)myelinated axon, C) near cell body, D) inferior olive neuron, (E) Purkinje cell.</p></figcaption>
</figure><p></p>
<h4 id="amplitude-and-frequency">Amplitude and frequency<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#amplitude-and-frequency">#</a></h4>
<blockquote>
<p>the amplitude of the action potential is independent of the magnitude of the current used to evoke it; that is, larger currents do not elicit larger action potentials. The action potentials of a givenneuron are therefore said to be all-or-none, because they occur fully or not at all</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>the intensity of a stimulus is encoded in the <strong>frequency</strong> of action potentials rather than in their amplitude. This arrangement differs dramatically from receptor potentials, whose amplitudes are graded in proportion to the magnitude of the sensory stimulus, or synaptic potentials, whose amplitude varies according to the number of synapses activated and the previous amount of synaptic activity.</p>
</blockquote>
<h4 id="phases-of-the-action-potential">Phases of the Action Potential<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#phases-of-the-action-potential">#</a></h4>
<ol>
<li><strong>Rising Phase (Depolarization)</strong>
<ul>
<li>Voltage-gated <strong>Na⁺ channels</strong> open.</li>
<li><strong>Sodium influx</strong> makes the inside of the neuron more positive.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Overshoot Phase</strong>
<ul>
<li>Membrane potential becomes <strong>more positive than 0 mV</strong>.</li>
<li>The inside of the neuron is now briefly <strong>positively charged</strong> compared to the outside.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Falling Phase (Repolarization)</strong>
<ul>
<li><strong>Na⁺ channels inactivate</strong>.</li>
<li>Voltage-gated <strong>K⁺ channels open</strong>, allowing <strong>potassium efflux</strong>, restoring a negative charge inside the neuron.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Undershoot Phase (Hyperpolarization)</strong>
<ul>
<li><strong>K⁺ channels remain open too long</strong>, making the neuron <strong>even more negative</strong> than the resting potential.</li>
<li>The neuron is temporarily less likely to fire another action potential.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>

<h4 id="the-relu-activation-function">The ReLU Activation Function<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#the-relu-activation-function">#</a></h4>
<p>The <strong>Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU)</strong> activation function used in artificial neural networks is loosely inspired by <strong>biological action potentials</strong>.</p>
<span class="katex-display"><span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.1076em;">f</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal">x</span><span class="mclose">)</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">=</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mop">max</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord">⁡</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord">0</span><span class="mpunct">,</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">x</span><span class="mclose">)</span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.1076em;">f</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal">x</span><span class="mclose">)</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">=</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mop">max</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.1076em;">f</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord">0</span><span class="mpunct">,</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">x</span><span class="mclose">)</span></span></span></span></span>
<ul>
<li><strong>Threshold-like behavior</strong>: Like a neuron, ReLU only activates when input <strong>exceeds zero</strong>, similar to how neurons fire only when a threshold potential is reached.</li>
<li><strong>No negative output</strong>: Biological neurons do not fire negatively; they either remain inactive (hyperpolarized) or fire a positive action potential.</li>
<li><strong>Computational efficiency</strong>: ReLU simplifies calculations, much like how neurons minimize energy use by firing only when necessary.</li>
</ul>
<p>However, real neurons exhibit more <strong>complex non-linearity</strong>, including:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Adaptation</strong> (changes in firing patterns over time).</li>
<li><strong>Refractory periods</strong> (temporary inactivation after firing).</li>
<li><strong>Subthreshold activity</strong> (graded responses below threshold).</li>
</ul>
<p>Thus, while ReLU is inspired by neuroscience, it <strong>oversimplifies real neural dynamics</strong>.</p>

<h4 id="nodes-of-ranvier">Nodes of Ranvier<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#nodes-of-ranvier">#</a></h4>
<p>We talk about the saltatory condution in <a href="/notes/firing-rate-based-network-models/">Firing-rate based Network models</a>.</p>
<p><strong>Nodes of Ranvier</strong> are gaps in the myelin sheath along the axon.
It takes from the name of the discoverer of these nodes.
Myelinated sections make the current flow 150x times faster compared to non Myelinated sections.
It is believed that myelinated section originated from a virus that joined the cell, and now they are in symbiosys.</p>
<p>They have a <strong>lower threshold</strong> for triggering an action potential, and <strong>higher concentration of Na</strong> molecules. The distance between each section is about 1.5mm. This is made so that the decrease in potential is not so much, due to the distance (the potential halves every around 0.4mm with normal membrane values).
The lower threshold helps to recreate the wave.</p>
<p>The voltage decay is lower in insulated versions, because the current is not lost to the environment (higher resistivity of the membrane thanks to the fatty part).
The myelination makes thin nerve fibers even faster than big squid axons! But they are quite expensive to maintain and build.</p>
<p>The propagation of voltage change within an insulator (the myelin) is fast, while the normal unmyelinated propagation is usually slower.</p>
<p>For example the large squid axon has a speed of 20m/s, while fibers of myelinated fybers have 100m/s speed.</p>

<h3 id="generating-and-action-potential">Generating and Action Potential<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#generating-and-action-potential">#</a></h3>
<p>Neurons communicate via <strong>electrical signals</strong> generated by the movement of ions across the <strong>cell membrane</strong>. These movements are mediated by two primary types of <strong>membrane proteins</strong>:</p>
<h4 id="ion-transporters-vs-ion-channels">Ion Transporters vs. Ion Channels<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#ion-transporters-vs-ion-channels">#</a></h4>
<ul>
<li><strong>Ion Transporters (Active Transporters)</strong>
<ul>
<li><strong>Move ions against their concentration gradient</strong> using ATP.</li>
<li>Example: <strong>Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase (Sodium-Potassium Pump)</strong>, which <strong>pumps 3 Na⁺ out and 2 K⁺ in</strong>, maintaining a concentration gradient.</li>
<li>Establish the <strong>chemical and electrical gradients</strong> necessary for neuron function.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Ion Channels</strong>
<ul>
<li>Allow <strong>ions to diffuse down their concentration gradient</strong> (passive transport).</li>
<li>Highly <strong>selective</strong> for specific ions (e.g., <strong>K⁺ channels</strong>, <strong>Na⁺ channels</strong>).</li>
<li>Can be <strong>voltage-gated</strong> (open/close based on membrane potential) or <strong>leak channels</strong> (always open).</li>
<li>The <strong>osmotic gradient</strong> drives ions, but the <strong>membrane potential</strong> balances the flow.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Chloride</strong> is used to hyperpolarize the cells.</p>
