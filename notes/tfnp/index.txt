2e:T12187,<h2 id="tfnp-and-its-subclasses-the-complexity-landscape-of-total-search-problems">TFNP and Its Subclasses: The Complexity Landscape of Total Search Problems<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#tfnp-and-its-subclasses-the-complexity-landscape-of-total-search-problems">#</a></h2>
<h3 id="context-and-motivation">Context and Motivation<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#context-and-motivation">#</a></h3>
<p>Standard complexity theory (P, NP, coNP) deals with <strong>decision problems</strong>. But many natural problems are <strong>search problems</strong>: given an input, find an object satisfying some property. When the object is <strong>guaranteed to exist</strong> (by a combinatorial or topological argument), we enter the world of <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">TFNP</span></span></span></span></span> — Total Function NP. This is the right home for Nash equilibria, local optima, fixed points, and gradient-descent stationary points.</p>
<p>The punchline of the snippet you sent: optimization (PLS) and equilibrium-finding (PPAD) are both total search problems, both believed hard, but <strong>structurally different</strong> — and their intersection <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">CLS</span></span></span></span></span> captures exactly the problems that are simultaneously "local-optimization-like" and "fixed-point-like," which is where gradient descent on smooth functions lives.</p>

class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathrm" style="margin-right:0.0139em;">poly</span></span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord">∣</span><span class="mord mathnormal">x</span><span class="mord">∣</span><span class="mclose">)</span><span class="mpunct">,</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.2222em;">V</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal">x</span><span class="mpunct">,</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0359em;">y</span><span class="mclose">)</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">=</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6444em;"></span><span class="mord">1</span></span></span></span>.</p>
<p><strong>Search NP</strong> (<span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">FNP</span></span></span></span></span>): the <em>functional</em> version. Given <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">x</span></span></span></span>, find some <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.625em;vertical-align:-0.1944em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0359em;">y</span></span></span></span> with <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.2222em;">V</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal">x</span><span class="mpunct">,</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0359em;">y</span><span class="mclose">)</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">=</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6444em;"></span><span class="mord">1</span></span></span></span>, or report none exists.</p>
<p>The reduction from search to decision is standard for <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">NP</span></span></span></span></span>-complete problems via self-reducibility, so for NP-complete <em>decision</em> problems, the search version is no harder. The world gets weird when we restrict to problems where a solution is <em>always guaranteed to exist</em>.</p>
<h4 id="total-function-np-mathsftfnp">Total Function NP (<span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">TFNP</span></span></span></span></span>)<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#total-function-np-mathsftfnp">#</a></h4>
<blockquote>
<p><span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord class="mrel">⊆</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mopen">{</span><span class="mord">0</span><span class="mpunct">,</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord">1</span><span class="mclose"><span class="mclose">}</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.6887em;"><span style="top:-3.063em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mbin mtight">∗</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2222em;"></span><span class="mbin">×</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2222em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mopen">{</span><span class="mord">0</span><span class="mpunct">,</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord">1</span><span class="mclose"><span class="mclose">}</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.6887em;"><span style="top:-3.063em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mbin <ol>
<li><span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0077em;">R</span></span></span></span> is polynomial-time decidable (verifier),</li>
<li><span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0077em;">R</span></span></span></span> is polynomially balanced (<span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord">∣</span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0359em;">y</span><span class="mord">∣</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">≤</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathrm" style="margin-right:0.0139em;">poly</span></span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord">∣</span><span class="mord mathnormal">x</span><span class="mord">∣</span><span class="mclose">)</span></span></span></span>),</li>
<li><strong>Totality</strong>: <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.8889em;vertical-align:-0.1944em;"></span><span class="mord">∀</span><span class="mord mathnormal">x</span><span class="mpunct">,</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord">∃</span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0359em;">y</span></span></span></span> such that <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal">x</span><span class="mpunct">,</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0359em;">y</span><span class="mclose">)</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">∈</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0077em;">R</span></span></span></span>.</li>
</ol>
</blockquote>
<p>The totality condition is the crucial twist. It means <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">TFNP</span></span></span></span></span> problems can never be <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">NP</span></span></span></span></span>-complete under standard reductions — if they were, then <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">NP</span></span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">=</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">coNP</span></span></span></span></span> (since "no solution exists" is vacuously false, hence trivially in <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">coNP</span></span></span></span></span>).</p>
<blockquote>
<p>[!note] Why TFNP is "between" P and NP
A <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">TFNP</span></span></span></span></span>-complete problem would imply <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">NP</span></span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2222em;"></span><span class="mbin">∩</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2222em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">coNP</span></span></span></span></span> has complete problems, which is widely believed false. So <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">TFNP</span></span></span></span></span> is "structurally" stuck in a middle ground — too easy to be NP-complete, too hard to be in P (presumably).</p>
</blockquote>
<h4 id="the-megiddopapadimitriou-insight">The Megiddo–Papadimitriou Insight<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#the-megiddopapadimitriou-insight">#</a></h4>
<p>Christos Papadimitriou's 1994 paper <em>On the Complexity of the Parity Argument and Other Inefficient Proofs of Existence</em> observed something deep: <strong>every problem in <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">TFNP</span></span></span></span></span> has its totality guaranteed by some combinatorial existence argument</strong>. Classify problems by <em>which argument</em> proves totality, and you get natural subclasses.</p>

<p>These are syntactic subclasses: defined by a <strong>canonical complete problem</strong> corresponding to the existence argument.</p>
<h3 id="pls-polynomial-local-search">PLS: Polynomial Local Search<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#pls-polynomial-local-search">#</a></h3>
<h4 id="definition-of-pls">Definition of PLS<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#definition-of-pls">#</a></h4>
<blockquote>
<p><span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">PLS</span></span></span></span></span> is the class of total search problems where solutions are <strong>local optima</strong> of a fitness function over a neighborhood structure.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Formally, a <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">PLS</span></span></span></span></span> problem is given by three poly-time circuits:</p>
<ul>

<li><span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.109em;">N</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal">x</span><span class="mpunct">,</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">s</span><span class="mclose">)</span></span></span></span>: returns the neighborhood of solution <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">s</span></span></span></span>,</li>
<li><span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.1076em;">f</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal">x</span><span class="mpunct">,</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">s</span><span class="mclose">)</span></span></span></span>: returns the fitness/cost of <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">s</span></span></span></span>.</li>
</ul>
<p>A valid output is any <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">s</span></span></span></span> such that <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" mtight"><span class="mord mtight"><span class="mord mtight">′</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">∈</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1.0019em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.109em;">N</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal">x</span><span class="mpunct">,</span><span class="mspace" class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.7519em;"><span style="top:-3.063em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 <ul>
<li><strong>Local Max-Cut</strong> under the FLIP neighborhood (Schäffer–Yannakakis 1991)</li>
<li><strong>Pure Nash equilibria in congestion games</strong> (Fabrikant–Papadimitriou–Talwos 2004)</li>
<li><strong>Stable matching with ties under local stability</strong></li>
<li><strong>Local optima of TSP</strong> under 2-opt / k-opt</li>
</ul>
</blockquote>
<p>The Fabrikant et al. result is gorgeous: <strong>pure Nash in congestion games = PLS-complete</strong>, because the Rosenthal potential function turns equilibrium-finding into local optimization.</p>
<h4 id="why-gradient-descent-lives-near-pls">Why Gradient Descent Lives Near PLS<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#why-gradient-descent-lives-near-pls">#</a></h4>
<p>Gradient descent on a smooth function is a <em>continuous</em> analog of local search:</p>
<ul>
<li>The "neighborhood" is an infinitesimal ball,</li>
<li>The "fitness" is the function value,</li>
<li>A "local optimum" is a stationary point (<span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.8889em;vertical-align:-0.1944em;"></span><span class="mord">∇</span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.1076em;">f</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">=</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6444em;"></span><span class="mord">0</span></span></span></span>).</li>
</ul>
<p>But <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">PLS</span></span></span></span></span> is purely combinatorial. The continuous version needs a different class — which is where <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">CLS</span></span></span></span></span> comes in.</p>
<h3 id="ppad-polynomial-parity-argument-on-directed-graphs">PPAD: Polynomial Parity Argument on Directed Graphs<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#ppad-polynomial-parity-argument-on-directed-graphs">#</a></h3>
<h4 id="the-lemkehowson-existence-argument">The Lemke–Howson Existence Argument<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#the-lemkehowson-existence-argument">#</a></h4>
<p>PPAD captures problems where existence is proved by the following lemma:</p>
<blockquote>
<p><strong>Lemma</strong>: In any directed graph where every vertex has in-degree + out-degree ≤ 2, if there is an <em>unbalanced</em> vertex (in-degree ≠ out-degree), then there is <em>another</em> unbalanced vertex.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>This is the directed version of the "handshake lemma." Think of the graph as a collection of paths and cycles. Each path has two endpoints — so unbalanced vertices come in pairs.</p>
<h4 id="definition-of-ppad">Definition of PPAD<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#definition-of-ppad">#</a></h4>
<blockquote>
<p><span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">PPAD</span></span></span></span></span>: the class of <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">TFNP</span></span></span></span></span> problems reducible to <strong>END-OF-LINE</strong>:
Given succinctly-represented circuits <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0576em;">S</span></span></span></span> (successor) and <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.1389em;">P</span></span></span></span> (predecessor) on an implicit graph over <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mopen">{</span><span class="mord">0</span><span class="mpunct">,</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord">1</span><span class="mclose"><span class="mclose">}</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.6644em;"><span style="top:-3.063em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mord mathnormal mtight">n</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span> with a known source <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6644em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord">0</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.6644em;"><span style="top:-3.063em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mord mathnormal mtight">n</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span>, find either:
(a) another source (in-deg 0, out-deg 1), or
(b) a sink (in-deg 1, out-deg 0), other than <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6644em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord">0</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.6644em;"><span style="top:-3.063em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mord mathnormal mtight">n</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span>.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The source <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6644em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord">0</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.6644em;"><span style="top:-3.063em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mord mathnormal mtight">n</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span> guarantees totality (it's the "first" unbalanced vertex; there must be another).</p>
<h4 id="why-nash-is-in-ppad">Why Nash is in PPAD<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#why-nash-is-in-ppad">#</a></h4>
<p>The classical proof of Nash's theorem uses <strong>Brouwer's fixed point theorem</strong>. Brouwer is the <em>prototypical</em> PPAD problem — Scarf's algorithm (1967) finds approximate fixed points by following an "improvement path" in a triangulation, which is exactly an END-OF-LINE walk.</p>
<p>The chain of reductions:</p>
<span class="katex-display"><span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.9805em;vertical-align:-0.2861em;"></span><span class="mord text"><span class="mord">Nash</span></span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel"><span class="mrel">≤</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t vlist-t2"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.1514em;"><span style="top:-2.55em;margin-left:0em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mord mathnormal mtight">p</span></span></span></span><span class="vlist-s">​</span></span><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.2861em;"><span></span></span></span></span></span></span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.9694em;vertical-align:-0.2861em;"></span><span class="mord text"><span class="mord">Brouwer</span></span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel"><span class="mrel">≤</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t vlist-t2"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.1514em;"><span style="top:-2.55em;margin-left:0em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mord mathnormal mtight">p</span></span></span></span><span class="vlist-s">​</span></span><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.2861em;"><span></span></span></span></span></span></span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.9694em;vertical-align:-0.2861em;"></span><span class="mord text"><span class="mord">Sperner</span></span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel"><span class="mrel">≤</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t vlist-t2"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.1514em;"><span style="top:-2.55em;margin-left:0em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing style="height:0.2861em;"><span></span></span></span></span></span></span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord text"><span class="mord">END-OF-LINE</span></span></span></span></span></span>
<p>So Nash <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.5782em;vertical-align:-0.0391em;"></span><span class="mrel">∈</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">PPAD</span></span></span></span></span>. The remarkable result of Daskalakis–Goldberg–Papadimitriou 2009 is the <em>converse direction</em> of completeness:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>[!note] Nash is PPAD-complete
Finding an <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">ϵ</span></span></span></span>-approximate Nash equilibrium in a 2-player normal-form game is <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">PPAD</span></span></span></span></span>-complete (Chen–Deng 2006 sharpened to exact 2-player).</p>
</blockquote>
<p>This is <em>the</em> milestone result of algorithmic game theory: it means Nash is, in a precise sense, <strong>as hard as fixed-point computation in general</strong>.</p>
<h4 id="ppad-complete-problems">PPAD-complete Problems<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#ppad-complete-problems">#</a></h4>
<ul>
<li><span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">ϵ</span></span></span></span>-Nash for 2-player games</li>
<li>Brouwer fixed point (approximate)</li>
<li>Sperner's lemma</li>
<li>Arrival problem on switching graphs (recent)</li>
<li>Market equilibrium in Arrow–Debreu with non-monotone utilities</li>
<li>Fair division: envy-free cake cutting (Deng–Qi–Saberi 2012, and later sharper)</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="cls-continuous-local-search-the-intersection">CLS: Continuous Local Search — The Intersection<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#cls-continuous-local-search-the-intersection">#</a></h3>
<h4 id="definition-of-cls">Definition of CLS<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#definition-of-cls">#</a></h4>
<p>Introduced by <strong>Daskalakis–Papadimitriou 2011</strong> to capture problems that are <em>simultaneously</em>:</p>
<ul>
<li>Local-optimization-like (PLS),</li>
<li>Fixed-point-like (PPAD).</li>
</ul>
<blockquote>
<p><span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">CLS</span></span></span></span></span>: total search problems reducible to <strong>CONTINUOUS-LOCAL-OPT</strong>:
Given Lipschitz-continuous functions <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" <h4 id="the-big-result-mathsfcls-mathsfpls-cap-mathsfppad">The Big Result: <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">CLS</span></span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">=</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">PLS</span></span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2222em;"></span><span class="mbin">∩</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2222em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">PPAD</span></span></span></span></span><a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#the-big-result-mathsfcls-mathsfpls-cap-mathsfppad">#</a></h4>
<p>For a decade it was open whether <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.8304em;vertical-align:-0.136em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">CLS</span></span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel amsrm">⊊</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">PLS</span></span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2222em;"></span><span class="mbin">∩</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2222em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">PPAD</span></span></span></span></span>. Resolved by:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>[!note] Fearnley–Goldberg–Hollender–Savani 2020 (STOC 2021)
<span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">CLS</span></span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">=</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">PLS</span></span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2222em;"></span><span class="mbin">∩</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2222em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">PPAD</span></span></span></span></span>.
Furthermore, <strong>gradient descent on a continuously differentiable function with Lipschitz gradient</strong> is <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">CLS</span></span></span></span></span>-complete.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>This is <em>the</em> characterization theorem: every problem solvable by some "gradient-descent-like procedure" is in <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">CLS</span></span></span></span></span>, and gradient descent itself is complete.</p>
<h4 id="cls-complete-problems">CLS-complete Problems<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#cls-complete-problems">#</a></h4>
<ul>
<li><strong>KKT points</strong> of smooth optimization (stationary points of constrained optimization)</li>
<li><strong>Approximate Brouwer fixed points</strong> with Lipschitz constraints</li>
<li><strong>Banach fixed points</strong> with explicit contraction</li>
<li><strong>Mixed Nash in congestion games</strong> (somewhere in this neighborhood)</li>
<li><strong>Tarski fixed points</strong> (related class)</li>
</ul>
<blockquote>
<p>[!tip] Practical implication for ML/AI
When you train a neural network with SGD, you are computing a <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">CLS</span></span></span></span></span> object. The convergence guarantees that practitioners take for granted (stationary points exist, gradient flow terminates) are <em>not free</em> — they require <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">CLS</span></span></span></span></span>-style totality arguments. Hardness in <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">CLS</span></span></span></span></span> means there is no expected poly-time algorithm finding <em>exact</em> KKT points in the worst case, even when one provably exists.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Most separations between TFNP subclasses are <strong>oracle separations</strong>: there exists an oracle <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">O</span></span></span></span> such that <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1.1201em;vertical-align:-0.1944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord"><span class="mord style="margin-right:0.0278em;">O</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel"><span class="mrel"><span These provide evidence (not proof) that the classes are distinct.</p>
<p>Key results:</p>
<ul>
<li>Beame–Cook–Edmonds–Impagliazzo–Pitassi 1998: oracle separation of <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">PLS</span></span></span></span></span> and <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">PPP</span></span></span></span></span>.</li>
<li>More recently, <em>unconditional</em> separations have been proven in restricted models (e.g., query complexity).</li>
</ul>
<h4 id="nash-strictly-contains-optimization-the-precise-statement">"Nash strictly contains optimization" — the precise statement<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#nash-strictly-contains-optimization-the-precise-statement">#</a></h4>
<p>The snippet says Nash <em>strictly</em> contains optimization "in this sense." What does that mean precisely?</p>
<ul>
<li>Both Nash and local optimization are in <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">TFNP</span></span></span></span></span>.</li>
<li>Local optimization (smooth, continuous) is in <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.8304em;vertical-align:-0.136em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">CLS</span></span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">⊆</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">PPAD</span></span></span></span></span>.</li>
<li>General Nash is <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">PPAD</span></span></span></span></span>-complete.</li>
<li><span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.8304em;vertical-align:-0.136em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">CLS</span></span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel amsrm">⊊</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">PPAD</span></span></span></span></span> is widely believed (with oracle evidence).</li>
</ul>
<p>So: <strong>continuous local optimization <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.7719em;vertical-align:-0.136em;"></span><span class="mrel amsrm">⊊</span></span></span></span> Nash</strong> (under standard complexity assumptions), but <strong>discrete local optimization (PLS) is incomparable with Nash (PPAD)</strong> — they intersect at <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">CLS</span></span></span></span></span>.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>[!question] Is every optimization problem a Nash problem?
Up to reduction: every <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">CLS</span></span></span></span></span> problem reduces to a Nash problem. But <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">PLS</span></span></span></span></span> problems generally <em>don't</em>. So the claim "optimization <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.7719em;vertical-align:-0.136em;"></span><span class="mrel">⊆</span></span></span></span> Nash" holds only for <em>continuous, smooth</em> optimization, not for combinatorial local search.</p>
</blockquote>
<h3 id="connections-to-your-work">Connections to Your Work<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#connections-to-your-work">#</a></h3>
<h4 id="multi-agent-ai-and-cooperative-ai">Multi-Agent AI and Cooperative AI<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#multi-agent-ai-and-cooperative-ai">#</a></h4>
<p>Your GovSim commons simulations are computing approximate Nash equilibria over repeated games with LLM agents. The <strong>complexity-theoretic backdrop</strong> is:</p>
<ol>
<li>Even if agents are myopic best-responders, computing the equilibrium they converge to is <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">PPAD</span></span></span></span></span> in general.</li>
<li>If your reward structure has a potential function (as in congestion games), convergence is <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">PLS</span></span></span></span></span> — local optima of the potential.</li>
<li>If you have a smooth utility landscape (as in mean-field games or continuous-action games), you are in <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathsf">CLS</span></span></span></span></span>.</li>
</ol>
<p>This means: <em>even with infinitely capable agents</em>, the equilibrium-finding step you require them to perform may be intractable. This is a hard lower bound on what "rational" cooperation between agents can achieve in worst case.</p>
<h4 id="mechanism-design-impossibility">Mechanism Design Impossibility<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#mechanism-design-impossibility">#</a></h4>
<p>The Gibbard–Satterthwaite / Myerson–Satterthwaite impossibilities are about <em>what</em> equilibria can exist. PPAD-hardness is about <em>finding</em> them. Even when a desirable equilibrium <em>exists</em>, no poly-time mechanism may extract it. Relevant for your GT-HarmBench reasoning about game-theoretic safety: hardness of finding safe equilibria <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.8889em;vertical-align:-0.1944em;"></span><span class="mrel"><span class="mord vbox"><span class="thinbox"><span class="rlap"><span class="strut" style="height:0.8889em;vertical-align:-0.1944em;"></span><span class="inner"><span class="mord"><span class="mrel"></span></span></span><span class="fix"></span></span></span></span></span><span class="mspace nobreak"></span><span class="mrel">≡</span></span></span></span> non-existence of safe equilibria.</p>
<h4 id="universal-composability-and-ppad">Universal Composability and PPAD<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#universal-composability-and-ppad">#</a></h4>
<p>Your ARIA proposal on UC frameworks and ZK reward certification has a parallel here: UC composes <em>cryptographic</em> hardness assumptions. There's an analogous story for <em>equilibrium</em> hardness — does composing two PPAD-hard games yield a PPAD-hard joint game? (Generally yes, but the constants matter.) Worth thinking about: ZK proofs of <em>equilibrium membership</em> would be a TFNP-style witness extraction.</p>

<h4 id="average-case-hardness">Average-case hardness<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#average-case-hardness">#</a></h4>

<h3 id="drawbacks-and-caveats">Drawbacks and Caveats<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#drawbacks-and-caveats">#</a></h3>
