1f:T1a049,<p>The DP (Dirichlet Processes) is part of family of models called <strong>non-parametric</strong> models.
Non parametric models concern learning models with potentially infinite number of parameters.
One of the classical application is unsupervised techniques like clustering.
Intuitively, clustering concerns in finding <em>compact subsets</em> of data, i.e. finding groups of points in the space that are particularly close by some measure.</p>
<h3 id="the-dirichlet-process">The Dirichlet Process<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#the-dirichlet-process">#</a></h3>
<p>See <a href="/notes/beta-and-dirichlet-distributions/">Beta and Dirichlet Distributions</a> for the definition and intuition of these two distributions.
One quite important thing that Dirichlet allows to do is the ability of assigning an ever growing number of clusters to data. This models are thus quite flexible to change and growth.</p>

<p>So they are jointly distributed as a Dirichlet distribution. And <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0813em;">H</span></span></span></span> is called the base measure and <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0037em;">α</span></span></span></span> is called the concentration parameter.</p>
<p>The base measure <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0813em;">H</span></span></span></span> represents our prior belief about the distribution of the data.
<span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0037em;">α</span></span></span></span> is called <strong>concentration parameter</strong>:  <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0037em;">α</span></span></span></span> is small, the resulting distributions tend to concentrate on fewer clusters, effectively encouraging sparsity. Conversely, a larger <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0037em;">α</span></span></span></span> allows for a richer, more diverse set of clusters.</p>

<h4 id="pólya-urns">Pólya Urns<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#pólya-urns">#</a></h4>
<p>We have urns with colored balls that can be drawn at random. If a ball is drawn it is doubled and put both back into the Urn.
We can prove that this is equivalent to the Chinese Restaurant Process, given a <strong>finite</strong> number of clusters (colors).
The important part is that this is exchangeable.</p>
<h4 id="hoppe-urn">Hoppe Urn<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#hoppe-urn">#</a></h4>
<p>This is a variation of the Pólya's urn. Here we have a special ball that when it's picked it is put back into the urn but also a new color is put back! Then this model is exactly the same as the Chinese Restaurant Process. It should be a nice exercise to prove this.</p>
<p>We can interpret the random variable <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">G</span></span></span></span> in De Finetti's theorem as a single sample from the an underlying Dirichlet Process. After we have sampled this (which is a prior, it is like we are sampling hyperparameters), then we use CRP to assign the points to the clusters.</p>
