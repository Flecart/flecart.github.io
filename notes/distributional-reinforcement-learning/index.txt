2c:T3a9a8,<h2 id="distributional-reinforcement-learning">Distributional Reinforcement Learning<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#distributional-reinforcement-learning">#</a></h2>
<h3 id="motivation-why-bother-with-the-whole-distribution">Motivation: Why Bother With the Whole Distribution?<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#motivation-why-bother-with-the-whole-distribution">#</a></h3>
<p>Standard value-based RL collapses the random return into a single scalar via expectation: <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">Q</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal">s</span><span class="mpunct">,</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">a</span><span class="mclose">)</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span><span class="mrel">=</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.2778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord mathbb">E</span><span class="mopen">[</span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0715em;">Z</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal">s</span><span class="mpunct">,</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">a</span><span class="mclose">)]</span></span></span></span>. The <strong>distributional perspective</strong> (Bellemare, Dabney, Munos, 2017) argues that this is information-destructive: two policies with identical means can have wildly different return <em>distributions</em> (bimodal vs unimodal, heavy-tailed vs concentrated), and modelling the full distribution yields:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Auxiliary learning signal</strong> — richer targets stabilise representation learning, even when only <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord mathbb">E</span><span class="mopen">[</span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0715em;">Z</span><span class="mclose">]</span></span></span></span> is used for control. Empirically the biggest reason it works.</li>
<li><strong>Risk-sensitivity</strong> — CVaR, distortion measures, robust planning all need <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.8333em;vertical-align:-0.15em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.1389em;">F</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t vlist-t2"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.3283em;"><span style="top:-2.55em;margin-left:-0.1389em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mord mathnormal mtight" style="margin-right:0.0715em;">Z</span></span></span></span><span class="vlist-s">​</span></span><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.15em;"><span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span>, not just <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord mathbb">E</span><span class="mopen">[</span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0715em;">Z</span><span class="mclose">]</span></span></span></span>.</li>
<li><strong>Better gradient information</strong> for function approximators (denser supervision than a single scalar regression target).</li>
</ol>
<div class="callout" data-callout="note">
<div class="callout-title"><span class="callout-icon" aria-hidden="true"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" viewBox="0 0 24 24" width="18" height="18" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M12 20h9"></path><path d="M16.5 3.5a2.12 2.12 0 0 1 3 3L7 19l-4 1 1-4Z"></path></svg></span><span class="callout-title-inner">The empirical surprise</span></div>
<div class="callout-content"><p>Distributional RL was originally motivated by risk-sensitivity, but the headline result of C51 was that it improved <em>risk-neutral</em> (mean-greedy) control on Atari. Modelling the distribution is a regulariser / representation booster, not just a risk tool.</p></div>
</div>
<h4 id="the-return-random-variable">The Return Random Variable<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#the-return-random-variable">#</a></h4>
<p>Define the <strong>random return</strong> along a trajectory starting at <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathnormal">s</span><span class="mpunct">,</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal">a</span><span class="mclose">)</span></span></span></span>:
<span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:1em;vertical-align:-0.25em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0715em;">Z</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.6644em;"><span style="top:-3.063em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mord mathnormal mtight" <h4 id="drawbacks-of-c51">Drawbacks of C51<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#drawbacks-of-c51">#</a></h4>
<ul>
<li><strong>Bounds required</strong>: <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.8778em;vertical-align:-0.1944em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.2222em;">V</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t vlist-t2"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.3175em;"><span style="top:-2.55em;margin-left:-0.2222em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mord mtight"><span class="mop mtight"><span class="mtight">m</span><span class="mtight">i</span><span class="mtight">n</span></span></span></span></span></span><span class="vlist-s">​</span></span><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.15em;"><span></span></span></span></span></span></span><span class="mpunct">,</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right:0.1667em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.2222em;">V</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t vlist-t2"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.1514em;"><span style="top:-2.55em;margin-left:-0.2222em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mord mtight"><span class="mop mtight"><span class="mtight">m</span><span class="mtight">a</span><span class="mtight">x</span></span></span></span></span></span><span class="vlist-s">​</span></span><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.15em;"><span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span> are hyperparameters. If returns can exceed them, mass clips and accuracy degrades. Tuning per environment is annoying.</li>
<li><strong>Discretisation artefacts</strong>: the support cannot adapt to the actual return distribution. Heavy-tailed or sparsely-distributed returns are poorly captured.</li>
<li><strong>Projection is heuristic, not Wasserstein-optimal</strong>: <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord">Φ</span></span></span></span> minimises a <em>Cramér-like</em> distance, not <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.9694em;vertical-align:-0.2861em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.1389em;">W</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t vlist-t2"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.1514em;"><span style="top:-2.55em;margin-left:-0.1389em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mord mathnormal mtight">p</span></span></span></span><span class="vlist-s">​</span></span><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.2861em;"><span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span>. The theoretical link between contraction (in <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.9694em;vertical-align:-0.2861em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.1389em;">W</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t vlist-t2"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.1514em;"><span style="top:-2.55em;margin-left:-0.1389em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mord mathnormal mtight">p</span></span></span></span><span class="vlist-s">​</span></span><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.2861em;"><span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span>) and algorithm (which optimises KL after a Cramér projection) is broken in practice.</li>
<li><strong>Asymmetric roles</strong>: probabilities are learned, locations are not — but it's the locations that encode magnitude information.</li>
</ul>

<h4 id="design-principle-summary">Design-Principle Summary<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#design-principle-summary">#</a></h4>
<ul>
<li><strong>C51's parameterisation is statistician-friendly</strong> (a proper PMF) but forces a heuristic projection and external bound choice.</li>
<li><strong>QR-DQN's parameterisation is geometry-friendly</strong> (atoms move freely) and aligns with Wasserstein theory, at the price of losing the "this is a normalised distribution" guarantee — the <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.8444em;vertical-align:-0.15em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.0278em;">θ</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t vlist-t2"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.3117em;"><span style="top:-2.55em;margin-left:-0.0278em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mord mathnormal mtight">i</span></span></span></span><span class="vlist-s">​</span></span><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.15em;"><span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span> could even appear unsorted (no monotonicity constraint), though they tend to sort themselves during training.</li>
<li>The asymmetry between them inspired <strong>dual representations</strong> later (IQN learns the inverse CDF as a function, FQF additionally learns the <span class="katex"><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.4306em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.1132em;">τ</span></span></span></span>'s).</li>
</ul>
