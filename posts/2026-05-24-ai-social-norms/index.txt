23:T216e,<p>It seems that for the long history of humanity, a big part of our moral abilities was crafted out evolutionarily (<a href="https://www.jstor.org/stable/j.ctv1g13jjn">Tomasello 2016</a>,<a href="#binmoreGameTheorySocial1994">Binmore 1994</a>).<br>
<span class="marginnote marginnote-nopunct" role="note" id="complex-systems-without-human-morality">Complex Systems without Human Morality<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1" href="#complex-systems-without-human-morality"></a></span> Yet the systems that we build don’t have morality in the human sense. They are mostly compliant with the code that we engineer into it, and don’t have a choice between cooperation and defection. They cooperate in the only ways humans want them to do, which is with the precision and unambiguity that computer code inherently has, by construction. <span class="marginnote marginnote-nopunct" role="note" id="unpredictability-of-modern-systems">Unpredictability of Modern Systems<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1" href="#unpredictability-of-modern-systems"></a></span> It’s the first time in human history that we cannot predict before deployment what kind of decisions a computer system can make in any given scenario, in the sense that knowing all the inputs does not necessary include known outputs in the system. <span class="marginnote marginnote-nopunct" role="note" id="the-wonder-of-intelligence">The Wonder of Intelligence<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1" href="#the-wonder-of-intelligence"></a></span> Yet their broad capability in most of the tasks that historically have always required deep human intelligence is indeed something new that has great value, and VCs, institutional investors and the broader society have grasped this importance. This begs the question of what and how best to extract this value from these models, and what use cases we expect them to solve.</p>
<h3 id="llm-expectations">LLM Expectations<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#llm-expectations">#</a></h3>
<p>The thing we need to discover is:<br>
<span class="marginnote marginnote-nopunct" role="note" id="defining-social-norms-for-llms">Defining Social Norms for LLMs<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1" href="#defining-social-norms-for-llms"></a></span> What are we expecting LLM agents to do? In other words, what are the social norms that we appoint to such systems? The general answer would be just “I expect the system to work”, yet what it “works” on is still something that we, as humanity, are discovering. It depends on what they actually <em>can</em> do, and we are very quickly expanding their capabilities and the ability to evaluate them in doing so.<br>
There are many ways that we expect them to <em>work</em>. Also take a look at <a href="/posts/on-norms-and-instructions/">On Norms and Instructions</a>.</p>
<p><span class="marginnote marginnote-nopunct" role="note" id="the-dream-of-creating-god-like-agents">The Dream of Creating God-like Agents<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1" href="#the-dream-of-creating-god-like-agents"></a></span> In the AI era with the AGI in view, it looks like we are expecting them to <strong>do everything a human can do, plus something more</strong> informed by the mastodontic amount of knowledge that they have. We are expecting them to improve more and more and get more autonomy than ever, and especially have some autonomous economic output. AI alone having abilities that no single human has. This means communicating and coordinating with other agents, with humans, and creating systems to access computer systems and use them well enough to reach some specific goal.</p>
<p><span class="marginnote marginnote-nopunct" role="note" id="ai-alignment">AI Alignment<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1" href="#ai-alignment"></a></span> Another expectation is following our command; this is the general problem of AI alignment (<a href="https://doi.org/10.1007/s11023-020-09539-2">Gabriel 2020</a>). We expect them to comply with our requests, even if our request is under-specified, incomplete in the <a href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.08426">Huang et al. 2026</a> sense. And as a society, we also expect them to refuse harmful requests that could help the user gather information to enact dangerous acts on society.</p>
<p><span class="marginnote marginnote-nopunct" role="note" id="specialized-knowledge">Specialized Knowledge<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1" href="#specialized-knowledge"></a></span> In more specific usages, particularly in workflows, we expect them to solve a very well-defined, limited use case, and only be specialized to do that. We thus incur in some specialisation-generalization trade-off: how much can we use an off-the-shelf foundation model to solve our problem, or do we need more specialized workflows, maybe some test time-based methods (<a href="http://arxiv.org/abs/2509.24510">Hübotter et al. 2026</a>)?</p>
<h3 id="ai-social-norms">AI Social Norms<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" hidden href="#ai-social-norms">#</a></h3>
<blockquote>
<p>Social norms anticipate competition in potentially disruptive situations and make it clear how individuals must behave in such situations in order to cooperate. ~<a href="https://www.jstor.org/stable/j.ctv1g13jjn">Tomasello 2016</a></p>
</blockquote>
<p><span class="marginnote marginnote-nopunct" role="note" id="the-problem-of-values">The Problem of Values<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1" href="#the-problem-of-values"></a></span> As a society, we are discovering what to expect from these models. The question of what norms to imbue models with seems still to be far, but eventually, it is one that needs to be engaged with. As models become more autonomous, they will get into the cooperative-competitive dilemmas that humans have implicitly struggled with in the past few millennia, especially if we have at some point autonomous economies à la <a href="http://arxiv.org/abs/2512.16856">Tomašev et al. 2025</a>.<br>
<span class="marginnote marginnote-nopunct" role="note" id="discovering-expectations-is-a-process">Discovering Expectations is a Process<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1" href="#discovering-expectations-is-a-process"></a></span> For some people, the process of discovering the expectations is quite dreadful. Tech leaders are talking about AI getting the job of most entry-level jobs. Some jobs were also thought to be among the most secure jobs from computer automation, such as lawyers, economists and other white-collar jobs.</p>
<p><span class="marginnote marginnote-nopunct" role="note" id="conclusion">Conclusion<a class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" tabindex="-1" href="#conclusion"></a></span> I don’t have a clear answer on how these norms should be defined and what we should build into them. It seems that lots of people have quite different ideas of what AI should do, and I don’t have a clear prescriptive framework to think about this topic. But the reality looks like the tools are already shaping how we are relating to technology, and as social media, also shaping how we will relate to other people. So giving some thoughts about this topic is an important one in this era.</p>
